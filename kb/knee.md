# Knee Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Knee injuries in climbing are more common than many climbers realise. Heel hooks, drop knees, high steps, and mantling place significant rotational and compressive forces on the knee. Most are soft tissue injuries that respond well to conservative management, but some — particularly meniscus and ligament injuries — require imaging to rule out surgical pathology.

**Disclaimer:** This information is for triage guidance only and does not constitute medical advice or diagnosis. Seek professional medical evaluation for any injury. In case of emergency call 911.

---

## Patellar Tendinopathy ("Jumper's Knee")

**Clinical name:** Patellar tendinosis / patellar tendinopathy
**Mechanism:** Heel hooks with high knee flexion load, high-stepping on steep terrain, repeated loading and unloading of the patellar tendon
**Location:** Inferior pole of the patella (just below the kneecap)

**Climbing-specific:** Heel hooking is the primary mechanism — pulling down through the heel with the knee in flexion creates massive patellar tendon load. Pain often spikes the next day rather than during the session.

**Symptoms:**
- Pain at the inferior pole of the patella
- Pain with knee flexion under load
- Pain that is worse the day after climbing rather than during
- Victorian test positive: single-leg squat with pain at 60 degrees of flexion
- Stiff and tender in the morning

**Recovery:** 8–16 weeks minimum. Chronic cases: 6–12 months.

**Rehab — Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) Protocol:**
- Spanish squats (isometric loading for immediate pain relief: hold 45 seconds x5)
- Leg press single leg
- Decline single-leg squat progression
- Isometrics first, then concentric-eccentric loading

**Return to climbing criteria:**
- Pain-free single-leg squat
- VISA-P score >80
- Pain-free heel hook on easy terrain before progressing

**Severity:** Mild: modify. Moderate (pain >4/10 with daily activities): physio within 1 week. Severe: avoid heel hooks entirely.
**Can continue climbing:** Modified — avoid heel hooks; footwork-focused slab or vertical
**Hangboard clearance:** Hangboard not a direct concern for knee
**Campus board clearance:** Avoid aggressive bouldering that requires deep knee flexion

## LCL Sprain — "Heel Hook Injury"

**Clinical name:** Lateral collateral ligament sprain
**Mechanism:** Heel hook creates strong external rotation force on the tibia while the knee is flexed — this loads the LCL and posterolateral corner structures
**Location:** Outer (lateral) side of the knee

**The most common acute knee injury in boulderers.** Often called the "heel hook pop."

**Symptoms:**
- Pain on the outer knee during or immediately after a heel hook
- Tenderness on the lateral knee
- Possible swelling
- Pain with resisted external rotation of the lower leg

**Recovery:** Grade I: 2–4 weeks. Grade II: 4–8 weeks. Grade III: 2–4 months.
**Red flags:** Significant instability, popping sound with immediate swelling, inability to bear weight
**Severity:** Grade I: mild. Grade II: moderate. Grade III: severe — requires evaluation.

---

## MCL Sprain (Medial Collateral Ligament)

**Clinical name:** Medial collateral ligament sprain
**Mechanism:** Drop knees, high steps with internal rotation, forced knee valgus
**Location:** Inner (medial) side of the knee

**Symptoms:** Inner knee pain, tenderness along the medial joint line, swelling, pain with valgus stress (knee pushed inward)
**Recovery:** Grade I: 2–3 weeks. Grade II: 4–6 weeks. Grade III: 2–3 months.
**Severity:** Grade I: mild. Grade II–III: moderate to severe.

## IT Band Syndrome

**Clinical name:** Iliotibial band syndrome, lateral knee pain
**Mechanism:** Drop knee position creates excessive valgus load and IT band tension, repetitive high-stepping, high-volume approach hiking
**Location:** Lateral femoral condyle (outer knee) — the IT band's impingement zone is at approximately 30 degrees of knee flexion

**Symptoms:**
- Sharp or burning pain on the outer knee that develops during activity and eases with rest
- Pain at 30 degrees of knee flexion (the impingement zone)
- Worsens as climbing session progresses
- Tender at the lateral femoral condyle
- May click or snap

**Recovery:** 4–8 weeks with hip strengthening and activity modification.

**Rehab:**
- Hip abductor strengthening (glute medius) — this is the primary fix
- Foam rolling ITB and TFL
- Avoid deep drop knee position initially
- Progress drop knee load gradually as hip strength improves

**Severity:** Mild to moderate
**Can continue climbing:** Modified — avoid deep drop knee; strengthen hip abductors

## Meniscus Tear

**Clinical name:** Medial or lateral meniscus tear
**Mechanism:** Drop knee position with rotation under load, heel hook with tibial rotation, twisting on a planted foot
**Location:** Joint line — medial or lateral

**Symptoms:**
- Joint line pain (inner or outer knee)
- Swelling that develops hours after injury (synovial effusion)
- Clicking, locking, or the knee catching
- Pain with deep knee flexion
- Positive McMurray test
- Difficulty fully bending or extending the knee

**Red flags:**
- Locked knee (inability to extend) — requires urgent orthopedic evaluation
- Significant effusion after low-energy mechanism in older climbers

**Recovery:** Partial/degenerative: 6–12 weeks conservative. Complete tear with locking: surgical repair 3–6 months or meniscectomy 6–10 weeks.

**Climbing note:** Drop knee on steep overhang is the highest-risk position for meniscus injury. Ensure adequate warm-up and avoid drop knee when fatigued.

**Severity:** Mild partial tear: moderate. Locking or significant effusion: severe — requires orthopedic evaluation.
**Can continue climbing:** Gradual vertical climbing only if pain is mild; avoid drop knee and heel hook

## Patellofemoral Pain

**Clinical name:** Patellofemoral pain syndrome
**Location:** Around or behind the kneecap
**Mechanism:** High-step moves, flagging, toe hooking on steep terrain, poor patellar tracking
**Symptoms:** Aching behind or around the kneecap, worse with prolonged sitting (cinema sign), descending stairs, squatting, crepitus under the kneecap
**Recovery:** 4–10 weeks with VMO strengthening and activity modification
**Severity:** Mild to moderate

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation (All Knee Injuries)

- Acute pop with immediate significant swelling (possible ACL or complex meniscus injury)
- Knee that locks and cannot be fully extended
- Significant instability — knee giving way during normal walking
- Rapid swelling within hours of injury (haemarthrosis)
- Inability to bear weight after injury
- Numbness or tingling around the knee or into the leg

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Avoid heel hooks and drop knees completely
- Avoid any move reproducing sharp knee pain
- Gentle range of motion within comfort; avoid forced end-range flexion or extension
- Ice 15–20 min (not directly on skin) and elevation in acute phase

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Reintroduce climbing on vertical terrain with controlled footwork
- Begin gentle strengthening: straight leg raises, mini squats in comfortable range, hip strengthening
- Reduce approach length and pace

**Return to full climbing:**
- Heel hooks are the last movement to reintroduce after LCL-region injury
- Test on easier heel hooks before aggressive ones
- Strong hip abductors reduce knee stress during heel hooks — include hip strengthening as maintenance
