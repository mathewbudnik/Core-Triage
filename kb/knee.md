# Knee Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Knee injuries in climbing are more common than many climbers realise. The movement demands of climbing — particularly heel hooks, drop knees, high steps, and mantling — place significant rotational and compressive forces on the knee. Most climbing-related knee injuries are soft tissue injuries that respond well to conservative management.

---

## Common Injury Patterns

### LCL Sprain (Lateral Collateral Ligament) — "Heel Hook Injury"
- **Location:** Outer side of the knee
- **Common in:** Heel hooking, especially aggressive heel hooks on overhanging terrain
- **Mechanism:** The heel hook creates a strong external rotation force on the tibia while the knee is flexed — this loads the LCL and the posterolateral corner
- **Presentation:** Pain on the outer knee during or immediately after a heel hook; tenderness on the lateral knee; possible swelling; pain with resisted external rotation of the lower leg
- **Note:** This is the most common acute knee injury in boulderers — often called the "heel hook pop"

### MCL Sprain (Medial Collateral Ligament)
- **Location:** Inner side of the knee
- **Common in:** Drop knees, high steps with internal rotation
- **Presentation:** Pain on the inner knee, tenderness along the medial joint line, possible swelling, pain with valgus stress (knee pushed inward)

### Patellar Tendinopathy ("Jumper's Knee")
- **Location:** Below the kneecap, at the patellar tendon
- **Common in:** Repeated high-step moves, mantling, board climbing with aggressive footwork
- **Presentation:** Pain below the kneecap, worse with loading activities (squatting, stepping up), stiff and sore after rest, better once warmed up but worsening with extended activity

### IT Band Syndrome
- **Location:** Outer knee, where the iliotibial band crosses the lateral femoral condyle
- **Common in:** High-volume climbing with repeated knee flexion and extension, particularly approach hiking and descent
- **Presentation:** Sharp or burning pain on the outer knee that develops during activity and eases with rest; may click or snap

### Patellofemoral Pain (Kneecap Pain)
- **Location:** Around or behind the kneecap
- **Common in:** High-step moves, flagging, toe hooking on steep terrain
- **Presentation:** Aching behind or around the kneecap, worse with prolonged sitting (cinema sign), descending stairs, squatting; may feel grinding or crepitus under the kneecap

### Meniscus Irritation
- **Location:** Inside the knee joint, medial or lateral
- **Common in:** Twisting loads under compression — drop knees, heel hooks with rotational stress
- **Presentation:** Joint line pain (inner or outer knee), possible clicking, locking, or giving way; swelling after activity; difficulty fully flexing or extending the knee

---

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- Acute pop with immediate significant swelling (possible ACL or meniscus injury)
- Knee that locks and cannot be fully extended
- Significant instability — knee giving way during normal walking
- Rapid swelling within hours of injury (haemarthrosis — blood in the joint)
- Inability to bear weight after injury
- Numbness or tingling around the knee or into the leg

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Avoid heel hooks and drop knees completely
- Avoid any move that reproduces sharp knee pain
- Gentle range of motion within comfort — avoid forced end-range flexion or extension
- Swelling control: elevation and ice (15–20 min, not directly on skin) in acute phase

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Reintroduce climbing on vertical terrain with controlled footwork
- Avoid aggressive heel hooks until pain-free
- Begin gentle strengthening: straight leg raises, mini squats within comfortable range, hip strengthening
- Reduce approach length and descent pace if hiking is aggravating

**Return to full climbing:**
- Heel hooks are typically the last movement to reintroduce after LCL or LCL-region injury
- Gradually test on easier heel hooks before aggressive ones
- Strong hip abductors reduce stress on the knee during heel hooking — include hip strengthening in rehab

**Key principle:** Knee injuries in climbing are often undertreated because climbers try to avoid footwork rather than address the underlying strength and mobility limitations.

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| LCL sprain Grade 1 | 2–4 weeks |
| LCL sprain Grade 2 | 4–8 weeks |
| LCL sprain Grade 3 | 2–4 months |
| Patellar tendinopathy (mild) | 4–8 weeks |
| Patellar tendinopathy (chronic) | 3–6 months |
| IT band syndrome | 4–8 weeks |
| Meniscus irritation (minor) | 4–8 weeks |
| Meniscus tear (significant) | Varies — may require surgery |
