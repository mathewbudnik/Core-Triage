# Ankle & Foot Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Ankle and foot injuries occur in climbers most commonly from bouldering falls, approach trail accidents, and aggressive footwork on small holds. Plantar fasciitis is a chronic issue unique to climbers who wear aggressively downturned shoes for extended periods. Early intervention dramatically improves outcomes for all foot and ankle injuries.

**Disclaimer:** This information is for triage guidance only and does not constitute medical advice or diagnosis. Seek professional medical evaluation for any injury. In case of emergency call 911.

---

## Lateral Ankle Sprain (Most Common Ankle Injury)

**Clinical name:** Anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) sprain — the most frequently injured ankle ligament
**Mechanism:** Landing from a boulder problem, slipping off a foothold, rolling the ankle on approach trail
**Location:** Outside (lateral) ankle — the ATFL connects the fibula to the talus

### Grading

| Grade | Description | Recovery Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| I | Stretch/microtear — full weight-bearing possible, mild swelling | 1–2 weeks |
| II | Partial tear — painful weight-bearing, moderate swelling and bruising | 3–6 weeks |
| III | Complete tear — significant instability, significant swelling and bruising | 6–12 weeks |

**Symptoms:**
- Lateral ankle pain
- Swelling and bruising on the outer ankle
- Pain with weight bearing
- Tenderness over the ATFL and CFL (calcaneofibular ligament)

### Ottawa Ankle Rules — X-ray Required If:

- Bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of the lateral malleolus (fibula)
- Bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of the medial malleolus
- Inability to take 4 steps immediately after the injury and in the ER/clinic
- If any Ottawa criteria are met, seek urgent evaluation before climbing

**Red flags:** Ottawa criteria positive, significant instability suggesting Grade III or fracture, neurovascular compromise
**Recovery:** Grade I: 1–2 weeks. Grade II: 3–6 weeks. Grade III: 6–12 weeks.

**Rehab priorities:**
- Early weight-bearing as tolerated (not full rest)
- Proprioception training is critical for climbers — single-leg balance progressions on progressively unstable surfaces
- Ankle strengthening (peroneals and tibialis anterior)
- Return to climbing: start on vertical terrain before overhanging; smearing before edging; no heel hooks until full stability restored

**Severity:** Grade I: mild. Grade II: moderate. Grade III: severe.
**Can continue climbing:** Grade I — vertical terrain only. Grade II–III: rest until weight-bearing is pain-free.
**Hangboard clearance:** Not directly affected; avoid if unable to stand pain-free on both feet
**Training modifications:** Maintain upper body fitness during ankle recovery

## Plantar Fasciitis

**Clinical name:** Plantar fasciitis / plantar fasciopathy
**Mechanism:** Aggressive shoe downsizing (toes forced into extreme plantarflexion), edging on small footholds for extended periods, high-mileage approaches

**Climbing-specific cause:** Wearing climbing shoes that are too small or too aggressively downturned for prolonged periods is the primary climbing-specific contributor. The plantar fascia is kept in sustained stretch for the duration of the session.

**Symptoms:**
- Heel pain worst with the first steps in the morning or after prolonged sitting
- Pain improves after warming up, then worsens with prolonged activity
- Tenderness at the medial calcaneal tubercle (inside of the heel, where the fascia attaches)

**Recovery:** 6–18 months for full resolution (notoriously stubborn). Early intervention dramatically improves prognosis.

**Rehab:**
- Calf raises with Alfredson heavy slow resistance protocol adapted for the plantar fascia
- Plantar fascia stretching: pull toes back toward shin before first steps in the morning
- Night splinting for significant morning pain
- Soft-tissue work: frozen water bottle or golf ball rolling on the arch

**Climbing modifications:**
- Remove climbing shoes between every attempt — do not wear them for extended periods
- Consider less aggressively downturned shoes for less severe routes
- Supportive footwear on approach; avoid flat shoes and flip-flops off the wall

**Severity:** Mild: self-manage with stretching and footwear changes. Moderate to severe (pain affecting daily walking): physio within 1 week.
**Can continue climbing:** Yes — with footwear modifications; remove shoes between burns
**Campus board clearance:** Not directly affected

## Peroneal Tendon Injury

**Clinical name:** Peroneal tendinopathy or peroneal tendon subluxation
**Mechanism:** Smearing on slab rock creates sustained ankle eversion loading; ankle inversion sprains that strain the peroneals eccentrically; repetitive eversion movements
**Location:** Posterior and lateral ankle, behind the fibula

**Symptoms:**
- Pain along the back and outer edge of the ankle
- Pain with foot eversion (pushing sole outward)
- Snapping sensation behind the fibula on ankle movement (subluxation)
- Tenderness posterior to the lateral malleolus

**Key differentiator from lateral ankle sprain:** Peroneal pain is posterior to the fibula (behind the ankle bone). ATFL pain is anterior and lateral.

**Recovery:** Tendinopathy: 4–8 weeks. Subluxation: may require surgical stabilization if recurrent or severe.
**Severity:** Tendinopathy: mild to moderate. Subluxation with snapping: requires evaluation.
**Can continue climbing:** Modified — avoid smearing and eversion-heavy footwork
**Modifications:** Avoid slab technique until pain-free; tape the ankle for support during return

## Medial Ankle Sprain

**Clinical name:** Deltoid ligament sprain (medial ankle)
**Mechanism:** Inward rolling with foot planted — less common than lateral sprain
**Symptoms:** Medial (inner) ankle pain, tenderness over the deltoid ligament, swelling
**Note:** Medial sprains are less common and often more significant — the deltoid ligament is strong, so a medial sprain should prompt imaging to rule out associated fracture
**Severity:** Moderate to severe — evaluation recommended
**Recovery:** 4–8 weeks

---

## Achilles Tendinopathy

**Clinical name:** Insertional or mid-portion Achilles tendinopathy
**Mechanism:** High-volume approach hiking especially on inclines, abrupt increases in hillwalking or trail running during climbing trips
**Symptoms:** Posterior heel or lower calf pain, stiffness in the morning that warms up, thickening of the tendon, pain reproduced by calf raise
**Recovery:** 8–16 weeks with structured loading program (Alfredson eccentric heel drop protocol)
**Severity:** Moderate — structured physio program significantly improves outcomes
**Can continue climbing:** Yes — climbing footwork not a direct mechanism; avoid approach hills

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation (All Ankle & Foot Injuries)

- Ottawa Ankle Rules criteria positive after a fall or roll — rule out fracture before climbing
- Inability to bear weight after injury
- Visible deformity at the ankle or foot
- Significant swelling within 30 minutes of injury
- Neurovascular compromise (cold foot, numbness, absent pulse)
- Achilles tendon: complete snap or sudden inability to plantarflex (push up on tiptoe) = Achilles rupture — surgical emergency

## Conservative Load Management

- Grade I ankle sprains: early protected weight-bearing and proprioception training
- Plantar fasciitis: remove shoes between attempts; stretch before first steps; strengthen calves
- Approach shoes matter: supportive, well-fitted approach footwear protects the ankle and foot between and after climbing
- Proprioception training is the most important factor in preventing ankle sprain recurrence

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Lateral ankle sprain Grade I | 1–2 weeks |
| Lateral ankle sprain Grade II | 3–6 weeks |
| Lateral ankle sprain Grade III | 6–12 weeks |
| Plantar fasciitis | 6–18 months (early treatment helps) |
| Peroneal tendinopathy | 4–8 weeks |
| Achilles tendinopathy | 8–16 weeks |
