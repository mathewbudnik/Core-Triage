# Triceps Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Triceps injuries in climbing come almost entirely from heavy lock-offs, mantling, and campus board work. The triceps extend the elbow against load — every hard lock-off held in cross-body or overhead positions, every mantle press at the top of a boulder, every campus board push-and-pull cycle is a triceps workout, whether the climber realizes it or not.

Most triceps complaints in climbers are insertional tendinopathy at the back of the elbow rather than mid-belly muscle strain. They overlap heavily with elbow tendinopathy and often warrant the same treatment approach. `needs_review`

## Common Injury Patterns

### Triceps Tendinopathy at the Olecranon (Back of Elbow)
- **Location:** Back of the elbow, at the bony point (olecranon) where the triceps tendon inserts
- **Common in:** Heavy lock-offs, mantling drills, campus board, boulderers with lots of explosive pulling
- **Presentation:** Aching at the back of the elbow during and after climbing. Tender to press directly on the triceps tendon insertion. Worse with resisted elbow extension and at the top of a hard lock-off.
- **Note:** Often paired with medial or lateral epicondylitis — the elbow as a whole is overloaded. Treat the whole elbow, not just the triceps. `needs_review`

### Long Head Triceps Strain
- **Location:** Back of the upper arm, often more proximal (closer to the shoulder)
- **Common in:** Hard lock-offs, dynamic catches, cross-body pulls — anything with sudden load on a near-extended arm
- **Presentation:** Sharp pain at the moment of injury, often described as a "pull" in the back of the upper arm. Tender to press on the muscle belly. Pain reproduced by resisted elbow extension and shoulder extension simultaneously.
- **Note:** The long head crosses both shoulder and elbow joints — that's why it gets loaded in dynamic shoulder-extension positions like cross-body lock-offs. `needs_review`

### Posterior Elbow Impingement
- **Location:** Back of the elbow at full extension
- **Common in:** Climbers who hyperextend at the top of lock-offs or during mantles
- **Presentation:** Pinching or sharp pain at the back of the elbow at full extension. Often pain-free in the middle of the range. May develop a small amount of swelling at the back of the elbow.
- **Note:** Often a structural issue (bone spur or thickened soft tissue) but begins as a load-management problem. Avoiding hyperextension and strengthening through controlled range usually resolves it. `needs_review`

### Triceps Overuse / Delayed-Onset Soreness
- **Location:** Diffuse back-of-arm soreness, usually bilateral
- **Common in:** Sudden volume increases on overhanging or campus-heavy training
- **Presentation:** General fatigue and soreness rather than a specific injury. Resolves within a few days with rest.
- **Note:** This is overtraining, not injury. Address volume, not rehab. `needs_review`

### Distal Triceps Tendon Rupture (Rare)
- **Location:** Back of the elbow with a sudden severe pop
- **Common in:** Maximal eccentric load on an extended arm — extremely rare in climbers
- **Presentation:** Sudden severe pain at the back of the elbow with an audible pop and an inability to actively extend the elbow against any resistance. Significant swelling and bruising follow within hours.
- **Note:** True surgical emergency in the same way distal biceps rupture is — requires prompt orthopedic evaluation. Very rare in climbing populations but documented. `needs_review`

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- Sudden severe pain at the back of the elbow with inability to extend the arm against resistance (possible distal triceps rupture)
- Visible deformity or asymmetry in the back of the upper arm
- Numbness or tingling in the ring and pinky fingers (ulnar nerve sits next to the triceps tendon)
- Pain that wakes you at night and is unrelieved by position change
- Symptoms that worsen progressively despite 4–6 weeks of conservative load management

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Drop heavy lock-offs, mantling, and campus board entirely
- Easy slab and vertical climbing — focus on bent-arm pulling, not extended-arm load
- Antagonist work: face pulls, band pull-aparts, scapular control
- Heat before activity, ice after if symptomatic

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Start isometric triceps loading: hold a 90-degree elbow position against resistance for 30s × 5 reps, daily
- Progress to slow eccentric tricep extensions (2–3 second lowering on a cable or band)
- Reintroduce moderate lock-offs at low intensity — bent-arm first, full lock-off later
- Continue antagonist work indefinitely

**Return to full climbing:**
- Lock-offs return in stages: bent-arm → moderate angle → full extension → loaded full extension
- Mantling and campus moves return only after pain-free moderate lock-offs are restored
- Tendinopathy is slow — expect 8–16 weeks before full intensity is safe
- Triceps issues often pair with medial or lateral epicondylitis — assess and address the whole elbow

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Triceps tendinopathy (mild) | 4–8 weeks |
| Triceps tendinopathy (moderate to severe) | 8–16 weeks |
| Long head triceps strain — grade I | 2–4 weeks |
| Long head triceps strain — grade II | 4–10 weeks |
| Posterior elbow impingement | 6–12 weeks |
| Triceps overuse / DOMS | 3–7 days |
| Distal triceps rupture | Surgical consult; recovery 4–6 months |
