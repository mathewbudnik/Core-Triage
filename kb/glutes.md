# Glute and Posterior Hip Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

The glutes drive hip extension and external rotation — two of the most loaded movements in modern climbing. Heel hooks, drop knees, wide stems, and aggressive rockovers all demand serious posterior hip output. Climbers are also notoriously weak in glute medius (the side glute), which controls single-leg stability and hip turn-in.

The clinical picture splits into two camps: structural overload (piriformis, deep gluteal, GTPS) and stability deficit (glute med weakness, SI joint dysfunction). Most respond well to load management plus targeted strengthening. `needs_review`

## Common Injury Patterns

### Piriformis Syndrome / Deep Gluteal Syndrome
- **Location:** Deep buttock, often referring down the back of the thigh (sciatic-like)
- **Common in:** Repeated external hip rotation under load — heel hooks, wide drop knees, stem moves
- **Presentation:** Deep buttock pain, possible referral down the leg (mimics sciatica). Worse with prolonged sitting, climbing in deep external rotation, or pressing on the piriformis. Often unilateral.
- **Note:** The sciatic nerve passes near (or through) the piriformis — that's why it can mimic disc-related sciatica. Imaging is usually negative; diagnosis is clinical. `needs_review`

### Gluteus Medius Strain or Weakness
- **Location:** Side of the hip, just below the iliac crest
- **Common in:** Climbers who don't train single-leg stability, those increasing slab volume rapidly
- **Presentation:** Lateral hip pain during and after climbing, especially on slab and during long single-leg holds. Trendelenburg sign (hip drop) on single-leg stance. Often pain-free at rest.
- **Note:** Glute med weakness is endemic in climbers. Often the underlying cause of "knee" pain (lateral knee from poor hip control) and IT band issues. `needs_review`

### Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome (GTPS)
- **Location:** Outer hip, over the bony prominence (greater trochanter)
- **Common in:** Compressive loading of the gluteal tendons — crossing legs, side sleeping, repeated hip drop during climbing
- **Presentation:** Lateral hip ache, very tender to press on the bony point. Worse with lying on the affected side, sitting cross-legged, or climbing with repeated hip drop in side-pull positions.
- **Note:** Was historically called "trochanteric bursitis" but the modern understanding is that the gluteal tendons (medius and minimus) are the primary issue — the bursa is secondary. `needs_review`

### SI Joint Dysfunction
- **Location:** Sharp or dull pain at the dimple just above the buttock (posterior superior iliac spine)
- **Common in:** Asymmetric loading — stems, drop knees, repeated hip drop, climbers with leg-length differences
- **Presentation:** Localized pain at the SI joint, sometimes referring into the buttock or back of the thigh. Worse with single-leg stance on the painful side, transitions like getting out of a car, and twisting.
- **Note:** Diagnosis is clinical (provocation tests). Often responds to glute medius strengthening plus pelvic stability work. `needs_review`

### Hamstring Origin Tendinopathy (Sit-Bone Pain)
- **Location:** Sit-bone (ischial tuberosity), where the hamstrings attach
- **Common in:** Heel hooking, prolonged sitting on hard surfaces
- **Presentation:** Deep ache at the sit-bone, worse with prolonged sitting and heavy heel hooking. See also `hamstrings.md`.
- **Note:** Often surfaces in the glute region but is technically a hamstring problem. Treat as proximal hamstring tendinopathy.

### Coccyx Pain (Tailbone)
- **Location:** Tailbone
- **Common in:** Falls onto the tailbone, prolonged sitting on hard surfaces (belay seats, gym pads)
- **Presentation:** Sharp or dull tailbone pain, worse with sitting and the transition from sit to stand. May persist for weeks to months after a fall.
- **Note:** Typically heals on its own with sitting modification (donut cushion). Imaging only if pain persists past 6–8 weeks. `needs_review`

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- New or progressive numbness, tingling, or weakness down the leg (possible nerve involvement)
- Bladder or bowel changes (see lower back red flags — cauda equina is an ER concern)
- Severe acute pain after a fall onto the buttock, especially with inability to bear weight
- Significant unexplained weight loss with hip or buttock pain
- Pain that wakes you at night and is unrelieved by position change

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Reduce climbing volume; drop heavy heel hooks and wide drop knees
- Avoid prolonged sitting (drives piriformis irritation and tailbone issues)
- Glute med activation: side-lying clamshells, single-leg bridges, lateral band walks (3 × 15, daily)
- Gentle piriformis stretching if symptoms allow

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Progress to single-leg glute bridges, lateral step-downs, and Bulgarian split squats
- Reintroduce easy heel hooks on large, comfortable holds at low intensity
- Address single-leg stability — climbers under-train this
- For GTPS: avoid compressive positions (hip adduction beyond neutral)

**Return to full climbing:**
- Reintroduce aggressive heel hooks last; start on big holds
- Maintain glute med work indefinitely — it's preventive, not just rehab
- Address technique issues that drive recurrence: dropping the hip excessively during high steps, asymmetric loading patterns

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Piriformis syndrome | 4–10 weeks |
| Gluteus medius strain (mild) | 2–4 weeks |
| Gluteus medius weakness (training fix) | 8–16 weeks of consistent loading |
| GTPS / lateral hip tendinopathy | 6–12 weeks |
| SI joint dysfunction | 4–8 weeks (with stability work) |
| Coccyx pain | 4–12 weeks (most resolve spontaneously) |
