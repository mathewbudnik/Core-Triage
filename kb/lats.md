# Lat and Teres Major Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

The latissimus dorsi is one of the most heavily loaded muscles in climbing. It's the primary mover for shoulder extension and adduction — every full hang, every steep pull, every dynamic catch loads the lats. The teres major works alongside the lat and is often involved in the same injuries.

Lat strains in climbers typically happen in two ways: a sudden eccentric overload (a dynamic catch that goes wrong, a foot cut that turns into an unexpected full hang), or chronic overuse from a sudden volume increase on steep terrain. They're slow to heal — expect weeks, not days. `needs_review`

## Common Injury Patterns

### Lat Strain (Acute)
- **Location:** Side of the back, under the armpit, sometimes referring to the inner upper arm
- **Common in:** Dynamic catches, dynos, foot cuts on steep terrain, sudden full hangs
- **Presentation:** Sharp pain at the moment of injury — often described as a "pull" or audible pop. Swelling is usually minimal. Pain reproduced by resisted shoulder extension or adduction. Stretching the arm overhead reproduces the symptom.
- **Note:** Acute lat strains are graded I–III. Most climbing strains are grade I–II and respond to load management. `needs_review`

### Teres Major Strain
- **Location:** Posterior shoulder / upper armpit area, often grouped with lat injury
- **Common in:** Same mechanisms as lat strains
- **Presentation:** Localized pain just below the posterior shoulder. Tender to press in the armpit. Pain with resisted shoulder extension and internal rotation.
- **Note:** Often misdiagnosed as a rotator cuff problem — the teres major sits adjacent and the symptoms overlap. `needs_review`

### Lat Tendinopathy at the Humerus Insertion
- **Location:** Front of the armpit, where the lat tendon wraps to insert on the upper arm
- **Common in:** High-volume steep pulling without adequate antagonist or mobility work
- **Presentation:** Aching at the front of the armpit, worse during and after climbing. Tender to press on the tendon insertion. Slow onset, slow recovery.
- **Note:** Less common than acute strain but increasingly recognized in high-volume board climbers. `needs_review`

### Posterior Chain Overuse
- **Location:** Diffuse soreness across the upper back, lats, and posterior shoulder
- **Common in:** Sudden volume spikes on steep terrain, board climbing camps, multi-day trips
- **Presentation:** General fatigue and soreness rather than a specific injury. Climbing feels harder than usual; recovery between sessions is incomplete.
- **Note:** This is overtraining, not injury. The fix is volume reduction, not rehab. `needs_review`

### Lat Trigger Points / Myofascial Pain
- **Location:** Tender points in the lat muscle belly that refer pain elsewhere — often into the inner arm or under the shoulder blade
- **Common in:** Climbers with poor lat mobility, those who skip overhead range work
- **Presentation:** Vague side-of-back pain that worsens with use. Referred pain patterns can mimic shoulder or elbow problems.
- **Note:** Self-massage with a lacrosse ball or foam roller is often diagnostic and therapeutic. `needs_review`

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- Acute pain with a visible defect or asymmetry in the armpit or upper back muscle bulk
- Significant weakness in shoulder extension or adduction (cannot pull arm down to side against resistance)
- Numbness, tingling, or vascular symptoms (cold arm, color change) — uncommon but possible with deep injury
- Pain that does not improve at all after 4–6 weeks of conservative load management
- Inability to lift the arm overhead

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- No hangboard, campus, or steep board climbing
- No dynamic catches or foot cuts on overhanging terrain
- Slab and easy vertical only, with minimal pulling load
- Gentle band external rotation and scap retraction (antagonist work)
- Heat before activity, ice after if swollen

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Reintroduce moderate hangs at body weight on a comfortable grip
- Add isometric pulls (rows held at midrange) progressing to slow eccentrics
- Mobilize the lat through full overhead range daily
- Easy vertical and mild overhanging climbing — no max efforts

**Return to full climbing:**
- Reintroduce dynamic catches last — the eccentric load is what re-injures
- Most lat strains take 6–12 weeks before full intensity is safe
- Consistent antagonist work (external rotators, scap retractors, serratus) reduces recurrence
- If the strain happened on a specific move, rehearse that movement at low intensity before returning to it on hard terrain

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Lat strain — grade I (mild) | 2–4 weeks |
| Lat strain — grade II (moderate) | 6–12 weeks |
| Lat strain — grade III (severe) | 3–6 months, may need surgical consult |
| Teres major strain | 3–8 weeks |
| Lat tendinopathy at humerus | 8–16 weeks |
| Posterior chain overuse | 1–2 weeks of reduced volume |
