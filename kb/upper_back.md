# Upper Back, Trap, and Rhomboid Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Upper-back tightness, mid-trap soreness, and rhomboid strain are some of the most common climbing complaints — and the most under-treated. Climbers spend long hours pulling toward the body, holding lock-offs, and tensioning through the scapulae. The result is a posterior shoulder girdle that's overworked and a postural set that creeps toward shrugged, internally rotated, and forward-headed.

These injuries are rarely structurally serious — but they drive shoulder, neck, and elbow problems downstream when ignored. Most resolve with load management plus active scapular control work. `needs_review`

## Common Injury Patterns

### Rhomboid / Mid-Trap Strain
- **Location:** Between the shoulder blades, often more on one side
- **Common in:** Steep pulling, hard lock-offs, repeated dynamic catches, sustained overhang sessions
- **Presentation:** Aching pain between the shoulder blades, worse during and after climbing. Tender to press on the muscle belly. Often described as a "knot" that won't release. May refer up the back of the neck.
- **Note:** Frequently bilateral but worse on the dominant pulling side. `needs_review`

### Upper Trapezius Overactivity / Tension
- **Location:** Top of the shoulder, base of the neck, sometimes referring into the head
- **Common in:** Hangboarders, climbers who unconsciously shrug under load, those with desk-job postural patterns
- **Presentation:** Tight, ropey upper traps that feel "permanently engaged." Tension headaches at the base of the skull. Tender to press. Worsens after hangboard sessions, board climbing, or long days at the computer.
- **Note:** Overactive upper traps usually mean the lower traps and serratus are under-recruited — a control problem more than a strength problem. `needs_review`

### Scapular Dyskinesis
- **Location:** Scapula (shoulder blade) movement pattern, not a single tender spot
- **Common in:** Climbers with overdeveloped lats and pecs vs weak rhomboids and serratus — extremely common
- **Presentation:** Visible winging of the shoulder blade during pulling or overhead reaching. Asymmetry between sides. Often paints the picture of "I can't quite engage my back when I pull." Drives shoulder impingement, biceps tendinopathy, and elbow problems if uncorrected.
- **Note:** Best identified by a clinician or coach watching from behind during pull-ups, hangs, and reaches. `needs_review`

### Levator Scapulae Strain
- **Location:** Pain at the angle where the neck meets the shoulder, on top of the scapula
- **Common in:** Roof climbing, overhanging belays, sustained head-up positions
- **Presentation:** Sharp or burning pain at the upper inner edge of the shoulder blade, worse with neck rotation toward the painful side. Often present alongside upper trap tension.
- **Note:** Easily confused with neck pain or trap pain — palpate the tendon insertion at the superior angle of the scapula. `needs_review`

### Thoracic Spine Stiffness (Hyperkyphosis)
- **Location:** Mid-back, often diffuse
- **Common in:** Long-time climbers with poor antagonist training, climbers who sit at desks
- **Presentation:** Stiffness in the mid-back, especially with extension. Difficulty getting full overhead reach without arching the lower back. Often pairs with shoulder mobility limitations.
- **Note:** Not strictly an injury but a contributing factor to most upper-back complaints. Daily thoracic extension drills are the highest-leverage fix. `needs_review`

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- New numbness, tingling, or weakness in either arm
- Severe sudden pain after a fall onto the back or shoulder
- Pain that wakes you at night and is unrelieved by position change
- Visible deformity or asymmetry of the shoulder blades after trauma
- Symptoms that worsen progressively despite load reduction over 3–4 weeks

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Reduce climbing volume; drop steep board sessions and hangboard repeaters
- Apply heat before sessions, gentle self-massage on the rhomboids and mid-traps
- Daily thoracic extension drills (foam roller mobilization, doorway openers)
- Cue active scap depression on every hang and pull

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Start prone Y-T-W raises (3 × 10 each, light or no weight)
- Band pull-aparts (3 × 15 daily)
- Serratus wall slides or plus push-ups
- Reintroduce moderate pulling — vertical first, then mild overhang

**Return to full climbing:**
- Maintain antagonist work indefinitely — climbers who skip it relapse
- Watch for shrugging during hangboard work; cue depression
- Address breathing pattern: chronic upper-chest breathing keeps upper traps engaged

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Rhomboid / mid-trap strain (mild) | 1–3 weeks |
| Rhomboid strain (moderate) | 3–6 weeks |
| Upper trap tension / overactivity | 2–8 weeks (depends on adherence to scap work) |
| Scapular dyskinesis | 8–16 weeks (a movement re-education project, not a healing one) |
| Levator scapulae strain | 2–4 weeks |
| Thoracic stiffness | Ongoing — daily mobility maintenance |
