# Red Flags — When to Seek Medical Care

Educational only. This information does not constitute medical advice or diagnosis. In case of emergency call 911.

---

## EMERGENCY — Call 911 or Go to ER Immediately

- **Cauda equina syndrome:** Bladder or bowel dysfunction (retention, incontinence, inability to feel urge) + lower back pain, saddle anaesthesia (numbness in groin/inner thighs), bilateral leg weakness — do not wait
- **Complete distal biceps rupture:** Sudden pop at the front of the elbow, immediate weakness, inability to supinate (turn palm up), Popeye deformity — surgical window is 2–3 weeks
- **Complete Achilles rupture:** Sudden snap at the back of the ankle, inability to push up on tiptoe — surgical emergency
- **Dislocated shoulder with neurovascular compromise:** Arm cold, pulseless, or numb after dislocation
- **Septic tenosynovitis (finger/hand):** Fever + extreme warmth + rapidly spreading redness and swelling in a finger — this is a hand surgery emergency
- **Visible bowstringing:** Tendon cord visible across the palm side of the finger — do not load before imaging

---

## Urgent — Seek Evaluation Within 24–48 Hours

- **Any audible pop** at a joint with immediate significant swelling, bruising, or inability to use the limb
- **Sudden weakness** — inability to raise the arm, extend the finger, or bear weight normally
- **Knee that locks:** Cannot be fully extended after a twisting injury or heel hook
- **Scaphoid mechanism:** Fall on outstretched hand + anatomical snuffbox tenderness — X-ray may be negative; CT or MRI required
- **Boutonnière risk:** PIP joint that cannot be passively extended to neutral after a jam or impact — requires splinting within 72 hours
- **Acute shoulder dislocation:** Any episode where the shoulder appears out of joint or reduces spontaneously
- **AC joint separation Grade III+:** Visible step deformity at the top of the shoulder
- **Progressive neurological symptoms:** Tingling, numbness, or weakness that is spreading or worsening

---

## See a Doctor or Physio Within 1 Week

- **Persistent radiculopathy:** Leg pain/tingling travelling below the knee from a lower back problem
- **Ulnar nerve symptoms:** Tingling in the ring and pinky fingers with medial elbow pain
- **TFCC injury:** Ulnar-sided wrist pain with instability or significant DRUJ looseness
- **High-grade pulley injury (suspected Grade 3):** Complete loss of pain-free flexion, significant swelling
- **SLAP tear suspected:** Deep shoulder clicking with overhead pain
- **Symptoms not improving after 2–3 weeks of load reduction:** Any region
- **Night pain:** Pain that consistently wakes you from sleep without a positional explanation

---

## Ottawa Ankle Rules — X-ray Required If

After ankle injury: bone tenderness at the posterior edge or tip of the lateral or medial malleolus, OR inability to take 4 steps immediately after injury and at evaluation. Seek evaluation before climbing.

---

## Do Not Ignore — Common Mistakes

- **Medial elbow pain:** Continuing to train through medial epicondyle pain is the most common way to convert a 6-week injury into a 12-month chronic tendinosis
- **Wrist after a fall:** Any fall on an outstretched hand warrants scaphoid screening even if initial pain seems mild — a missed fracture risks avascular necrosis
- **Finger pop:** A pop in the finger during climbing should be treated conservatively (no loading) and evaluated — not taped up and climbed through
- **Saddle symptoms:** Any bowel/bladder change with back pain = 911. Not tomorrow. Now.

---

## Severity Classification (All Injuries)

- **Mild:** Pain <4/10, no functional loss, manageable with self-care and load reduction
- **Moderate:** Pain 4–7/10, functional limitation, see a healthcare provider within 1 week
- **Severe:** Pain >7/10, significant functional loss or neurological symptoms, see a provider within 24–48 hours
- **Emergency:** Any red flag symptom above — ER or 911 immediately
