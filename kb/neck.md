# Neck (Cervical) Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Neck injuries are uncommon in climbing but not rare — roof climbing, sustained overhead positions, and falls place significant demands on the cervical spine. The neck houses the spinal cord and the nerve roots supplying both arms, which makes neurological symptom recognition critical. Any neck injury accompanied by arm tingling, numbness, weakness, or electric sensations requires prompt professional evaluation.

---

## Common Injury Patterns

### Cervical Muscle Strain
- **Location:** Neck muscles — suboccipital, trapezius, sternocleidomastoid, deep cervical extensors
- **Common in:** Sustained overhead positions (roof climbing), awkward head positions on steep terrain, sudden twisting loads
- **Presentation:** Generalised neck stiffness and ache, often worse the day after a session, pain with end-range rotation or extension
- **Note:** Most common cervical complaint in climbers — usually benign and self-limiting with rest and load modification

### Cervical Facet Irritation
- **Location:** Facet (zygapophyseal) joints of the cervical spine
- **Common in:** Repeated sustained neck extension on overhangs and roofs, high-volume climbing with poor positioning
- **Presentation:** Deep, aching neck pain, stiffness worst in the morning, pain with extension and rotation, may refer to the shoulder or upper back
- **Note:** Often indistinguishable from muscle strain clinically — imaging may be required if persistent

### Cervical Disc Irritation
- **Location:** Intervertebral discs of the cervical spine (C4–C7 most common)
- **Common in:** Sudden loading in a flexed or extended neck position, falls, whipping dynamic moves
- **Presentation:** Neck pain with possible radiation into the arm, stiffness, may worsen with flexion or extension depending on disc level
- **Note:** Requires evaluation if symptoms persist more than 2–3 weeks or if arm symptoms develop

### Cervical Radiculopathy
- **Location:** Compressed or irritated nerve root exiting the cervical spine
- **Common in:** Associated with disc herniation or facet overgrowth; can follow acute injury or develop gradually
- **Presentation:** Sharp, shooting, or electric pain radiating from the neck into the arm, following a specific dermatomal pattern; may include numbness, tingling, or weakness in the arm or hand
- **Note:** Requires evaluation — neurological symptoms in the arm from neck involvement need professional assessment

---

## Red Flags — Seek Urgent Evaluation

- Arm numbness, tingling, or weakness following neck pain or injury
- Electric or shock-like sensations radiating from the neck into the arms or legs
- Clumsy hands or difficulty with fine motor tasks
- Neck pain following a significant fall or trauma
- Progressive worsening of arm neurological symptoms
- Any suggestion of bilateral arm or leg symptoms from a neck problem

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Avoid positions that reproduce arm symptoms — these indicate nerve root involvement and require professional review
- Reduce roof climbing and sustained overhead positions
- Gentle range of motion as tolerated; avoid forcing end range
- Ice or heat for symptom relief during the acute phase

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Restore cervical range of motion through gentle, pain-free movement
- Strengthen deep cervical flexors: chin tucks, neck retraction exercises
- Scapular and shoulder strengthening to reduce demands on the cervical spine
- Gradually reintroduce climbing — slab and vertical terrain first

**Return to full climbing:**
- Full pain-free range of motion in all planes before returning to steep terrain
- No arm neurological symptoms at rest or with activity
- Roof climbing and overhead terrain reintroduced last
- If any arm symptoms were present, professional clearance is recommended before returning to full training

---

## Training Modifications During Recovery

**Permitted:**
- Easy slab and vertical climbing with neutral head positioning
- Hangboard training if neck is in a comfortable position and no arm symptoms
- Shoulder, scapular, and core strengthening
- Lower body training

**Avoid:**
- Roof climbing and sustained neck extension positions
- Inverted climbing (e.g. coffin rests, steep roof sequences)
- Campus board — impact loads on the cervical spine
- Any movement that reproduces arm tingling, numbness, or weakness

---

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Cervical muscle strain | 1–3 weeks |
| Cervical facet irritation | 2–6 weeks |
| Cervical disc irritation (no arm symptoms) | 4–8 weeks |
| Cervical radiculopathy | 6–12 weeks (variable) |

---

## When Conservative Management Is Not Enough

Seek evaluation if:
- Arm neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, weakness) are present or worsening
- Pain is not improving after 2–3 weeks of genuine load reduction
- Neck pain is severe or associated with headache and visual changes (cervicogenic headache)
- Any balance disturbance, difficulty walking, or loss of bowel/bladder control (these are emergencies)
