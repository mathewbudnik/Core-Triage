# Finger Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Finger injuries are the most common climbing-related injury, accounting for over 30% of all climbing injuries. The majority are overuse injuries affecting the flexor tendon pulley system, though acute injuries from dynamic moves and pockets are also common. Most finger injuries respond well to conservative management with appropriate load modification.

---

## Anatomy Basics

The finger flexor tendons run through a series of fibrous rings called pulleys (A1–A5). The A2 and A4 pulleys are most commonly injured in climbers. These pulleys hold the tendon close to the bone, enabling efficient force transfer during gripping.

---

## Common Injury Patterns

### Pulley Strain / Rupture (A2, A4)
- **Location:** Palm side of the finger, most often the ring finger or middle finger at the A2 region (base of proximal phalanx)
- **Common in:** Hard crimping, pockets, dynamic catching
- **Grades:**
  - Grade 1: Mild strain, no structural disruption — pain with load, no bowstringing
  - Grade 2: Partial tear — pain at rest, mild to moderate swelling, no bowstringing
  - Grade 3: Complete rupture — significant swelling, possible bowstringing (visible tendon displacement), marked weakness
  - Grade 4: Multiple pulley rupture or combined with lumbrical injury
- **Presentation:** Pain on the palm side of the finger; may feel a pop or snap at time of injury; pain with resisted finger flexion; tenderness on palpation of the pulley region

### Flexor Tendon Irritation
- **Location:** Along the tendon sheath from palm to fingertip
- **Common in:** High-volume training, repeated gripping over time
- **Presentation:** Diffuse tenderness along the tendon, pain worse with repeated gripping, stiffness after rest, may feel gritty or grinding sensation

### Collateral Ligament Sprain
- **Location:** Sides of the finger joints (PIP or DIP joints)
- **Common in:** Sideways loading, taping accidents, gastons, sidepulls
- **Presentation:** Pain on the side of the joint, swelling, tenderness, possible instability feeling

### Joint Capsule Irritation
- **Location:** Around the PIP (proximal interphalangeal) or DIP joint
- **Common in:** Chronic high-volume loading, repeated joint impact
- **Presentation:** Joint swelling (may persist for months), stiffness especially in the morning, pain near the joint line

### Lumbrical Tear
- **Location:** Deep in the palm, often the ring finger lumbrical
- **Common in:** Asymmetric pocket loading (one finger in pocket, others extended)
- **Presentation:** Deep palm pain that worsens when the fingers are extended while flexing at the MCP joint — a distinctive sign

---

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- Audible pop or snap with immediate significant swelling or bruising
- Visible bowstringing of the tendon (visible cord across the palm side of the finger)
- Inability to flex or extend the finger
- Marked weakness or rapidly worsening function
- Numbness or tingling in the finger
- Suspected Grade 3+ pulley rupture

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Avoid all moves that reproduce sharp pain
- Full crimp and pocket grips should be avoided
- Open-hand grip on easier terrain may be tolerated if pain stays below 3/10
- Gentle range of motion exercises (flexion and extension within pain limits)
- Buddy taping to the adjacent finger can provide comfort (not a structural fix)

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks, depending on grade):**
- Gradual reintroduction of load — start with easy jugs and sloper-style holds
- Avoid pockets and full crimp until pain-free through range
- Add gentle isometric finger flexion against minimal resistance
- Progress to half crimp before full crimp

**Return to full training:**
- Increase only one variable at a time (volume, then intensity)
- Use the "24-hour rule" — no significant pain increase during or within 24 hours of training
- Avoid maximum effort hangboard sessions until pain-free for 4+ weeks

**Taping:**
- H-tape (ring taping) over the A2 pulley region may provide proprioceptive benefit and mild mechanical support
- Does not replace load management
- Should not be used to train through pain
