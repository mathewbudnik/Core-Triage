# Finger & Hand Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Finger injuries are the most common climbing injury, accounting for over 30% of all cases. The flexor tendon pulley system bears enormous load during crimping. Understanding the specific injury type and grade is critical — the difference between Grade 1 and Grade 3 is the difference between 2 weeks and 6 months off.

**Disclaimer:** This information is for triage guidance only and does not constitute medical advice or diagnosis. Seek professional medical evaluation for any injury. In case of emergency call 911.

---

## Anatomy Basics

The finger flexor tendons run through five fibrous rings (A1–A5 pulleys). A2 (base of proximal phalanx) and A4 (middle phalanx) bear the greatest load during crimping. When a pulley ruptures, the tendon may bowstring away from the bone — visible on ultrasound or sometimes with the naked eye in severe cases.

---

## A2 Pulley Rupture (Most Common)

**Clinical name:** Annular pulley rupture, A2 most common (ring finger, middle finger)
**Mechanism:** Full crimp grip, dynamic catches, overloading small holds
**Location:** Palm-side of finger at the base of the proximal phalanx

### Schöffl Grading System

| Grade | Description | Recovery Estimate |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Sprain, no structural damage, mild pain with climbing | 1–3 weeks |
| 2 | Partial rupture, moderate pain, slight bowstringing on ultrasound | 4–8 weeks |
| 3 | Complete single-pulley rupture, significant bowstringing on ultrasound | 8–16 weeks |
| 4 | Multiple pulley rupture or combined injury (lumbrical, collateral, volar plate) | 4–6+ months, may require surgical consult |

**Symptoms:**
- Audible pop at moment of injury
- Acute pain at the base of the finger (palm side)
- Swelling, pain with passive extension
- Pain on palpation of the proximal phalanx (A2 region)
- Bowstringing visible on ultrasound in Grade 2+

**Differentiating A2 from A4:** A2 pain is at the base of the finger (proximal phalanx). A4 pain is at the middle segment of the finger (middle phalanx).

**Red flags:**
- Bowstringing visible to the naked eye (Grade 3–4)
- Inability to flex the finger
- Neurovascular compromise (finger goes cold or numb)
- Suspected Grade 4 — requires imaging and specialist evaluation

**Rehab phases:**
- **Acute (0–2 weeks):** Active ROM exercises, buddy taping, no loading of the injured finger, edema control
- **Subacute (2–8 weeks):** Progressive tendon gliding, massage, isometric open-hand loading only
- **Return to sport (8–16 weeks for Grade 3):** Hangboard open-hand protocol starting at 50% bodyweight; no full crimp until fully pain-free

**Return to climbing criteria:** Pain-free passive extension, pain-free palpation over pulley, negative bowstring test on ultrasound, passed progressive loading protocol

**Climbing modifications on return:** Open-hand grip only; avoid crimp position for 3 months post Grade 3; tape A2 region; avoid small holds and one-arm lock-offs

**Severity classification:**
- Grade 1: Mild — manageable with self-care and load reduction
- Grade 2: Moderate — see sports physio within 1 week
- Grade 3: Severe — see sports physio within 48 hours, imaging recommended
- Grade 4: Emergency consult — surgical evaluation required

**Hangboard clearance:** Grade 1: 2 weeks open-hand. Grade 2: 4–6 weeks open-hand. Grade 3: 8+ weeks open-hand before any loading
**Campus board clearance:** Grade 1: 4 weeks. Grade 2: 8 weeks. Grade 3: 4–6 months, last activity to return

## A4 Pulley Rupture

**Clinical name:** A4 annular pulley rupture
**Mechanism:** Sport climbing at limit grade, lock-off positions, pocket climbing
**Location:** Middle phalanx — pain is mid-finger, not at the base
**Most common finger:** Middle finger

**Symptoms:** Pain at the middle segment of the finger, pain with passive DIP extension, tenderness on palpation of the middle phalanx
**Key differentiator from A2:** A4 pain is mid-finger; A2 pain is at the base near the palm

**Recovery:** Partial: 6–12 weeks. Complete: up to 20 weeks.
**Note:** Less common than A2 but recovery is often longer due to poorer vascularity at the A4 location. Responds to the same rehab protocol as A2.

---

## Flexor Tendon Tenosynovitis

**Clinical name:** Flexor digitorum superficialis/profundus tenosynovitis
**Mechanism:** Overuse, high training volume, inadequate recovery, sustained crimping over weeks
**Key differentiator from pulley:** Pain is diffuse along the entire finger, not focal at one pulley point. No bowstringing. Swelling is along the whole finger, not just at one segment.

**Symptoms:**
- Diffuse swelling along the entire finger
- Warmth
- Morning stiffness that loosens with movement
- Crepitus (gritty feeling) with flexion
- Pain pattern: worse after rest → improves with warm-up → worsens again with prolonged activity

**Red flags:** Fever + extreme warmth + rapid spreading swelling = possible septic tenosynovitis — medical emergency, seek ER immediately
**Recovery:** 4–10 weeks with complete rest from loading
**Treatment:** Complete rest from climbing, NSAIDs if appropriate, contrast baths, tendon gliding exercises only, no loading until pain-free

**Severity:** Moderate — see a doctor within 1 week if symptoms do not improve with 1–2 weeks rest
**Hangboard clearance:** No loading until fully pain-free with active ROM
**Can continue climbing:** No — requires complete rest from loading until pain-free

---

## Bowstringing (Multiple Pulley Failure)

**Clinical name:** Flexor tendon bowstringing from multiple pulley failure
**Mechanism:** Sequential pulley failure — usually untreated A2 rupture combined with A3 or A4 damage
**Symptoms:** Visible tendon displacement away from the bone during finger flexion, loss of grip strength, mechanical inefficiency. May be painless in chronic cases.

**Diagnosis:** Confirmed by ultrasound or MRI
**Red flags:** ANY visible bowstringing = requires imaging and surgical consultation before return to climbing
**Recovery:** Often requires surgical reconstruction. 6–12 months post-op.
**Severity:** Emergency consult required

---

## Boutonnière Deformity (Central Slip Rupture)

**Clinical name:** Central slip rupture with lateral band displacement
**Mechanism:** Forced PIP flexion against resistance — jamming fingers in a crack, catching a fall with open hand, impact to the dorsal PIP joint

**Symptoms:**
- PIP joint pain
- Inability to fully extend the PIP joint
- Hyperextension of the DIP joint (compensatory)
- Progressive deformity if untreated

**Red flags:** Any PIP joint that cannot be passively extended to neutral requires splinting within 72 hours to prevent permanent deformity
**Treatment:** Immediate PIP splinting in full extension for 6–8 weeks continuous. Do not remove the splint during treatment. DIP flexion exercises are permitted and encouraged.

**Recovery:** 8–12 weeks with proper splinting. Surgical repair required if diagnosed late.
**Return to climbing:** Splint the PIP in extension during climbing for additional 6 weeks after active treatment
**Severity:** Severe — requires professional evaluation within 24–48 hours. Do not attempt self-treatment without clinical guidance.

---

## Lumbrical Tear

**Location:** Deep in the palm, most commonly the ring finger lumbrical
**Mechanism:** Asymmetric pocket loading — one finger in a pocket while others are extended creates a shearing force across the lumbrical

**Symptoms:** Deep palm pain that worsens when fingers are extended while flexing at the MCP joint — this distinctive pattern (worsens on extension, not just flexion) differentiates it from pulley injuries
**Recovery:** 4–8 weeks. Conservative management. Avoid pockets and asymmetric finger loading.
**Severity:** Moderate — reduce load, see physio if not improving within 2 weeks

---

## Collateral Ligament Sprain

**Location:** Sides of the PIP or DIP joints
**Mechanism:** Sideways loading, sidepull holds, gastons, jams in crack climbing
**Symptoms:** Pain on the side of the joint, swelling, tenderness, possible instability feeling
**Recovery:** Grade 1: 2–4 weeks. Grade 2: 4–8 weeks. Grade 3: 8–16 weeks (surgical consult if complete tear)
**Severity:** Mild to moderate depending on grade

---

## Joint Capsule Irritation

**Location:** Around the PIP or DIP joint
**Mechanism:** Chronic high-volume loading, repeated joint compression
**Symptoms:** Joint swelling (may persist months), morning stiffness, pain near the joint line, may remain swollen even after climbing stops
**Recovery:** Weeks to months — chronic capsular swelling is common and often persistent
**Severity:** Mild — manageable with load reduction

---

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation (All Finger Injuries)

- Audible pop with immediate significant swelling or bruising
- Visible bowstringing (tendon cord visible across palm side of finger)
- Inability to flex or extend the finger
- Marked weakness, rapidly worsening function
- Numbness or tingling in the finger
- PIP joint that cannot be passively extended — possible Boutonnière deformity
- Suspected Grade 3+ pulley rupture
- Fever combined with warmth and spreading swelling (possible infection)

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (0–2 weeks):**
- Avoid all moves that reproduce sharp pain
- Full crimp and pocket grips should be avoided
- Open-hand grip on easy terrain may be tolerated if pain stays below 3/10
- Gentle ROM within pain limits (active flexion and extension)
- Buddy taping to the adjacent finger for comfort (not a structural repair)

**Subacute phase (2–8 weeks depending on grade):**
- Gradual reintroduction of load — start with jugs and slopers
- Avoid pockets and full crimp until pain-free through full range
- Add gentle isometric open-hand loading against minimal resistance
- Progress from open hand to half crimp before full crimp

**Return to full training:**
- Use the 24-hour rule: no significant pain during or within 24 hours of training
- Avoid max-effort hangboard until pain-free for 4+ weeks
- Increase one variable at a time: volume, then intensity

**Taping:** H-tape (ring taping) over the A2 pulley provides mild mechanical support and proprioceptive feedback. Does not replace load management. Never tape to train through pain.
