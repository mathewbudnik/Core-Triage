# Hip Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Hip injuries in climbing are increasingly recognised as training volumes increase and climbers tackle more gymnastic movement styles. High steps, heel hooks, toe hooks, and wide bridging moves place significant demands on hip flexors, external rotators, the hip joint capsule, and surrounding musculature. Hip issues are often underdiagnosed because they can mimic lower back pain.

---

## Common Injury Patterns

### Hip Flexor Strain (Iliopsoas)
- **Location:** Front of the hip, deep groin area
- **Common in:** High stepping, aggressive rockover moves, compression climbing with repeated hip flexion
- **Presentation:** Deep groin or front-of-hip ache during high steps, pain with resisted hip flexion, tenderness in the groin region, possible clicking sensation (snapping hip)
- **Note:** Often develops gradually — climbers initially notice fatigue in the hip before pain develops

### Piriformis Syndrome / Deep Gluteal Syndrome
- **Location:** Deep buttock, piriformis muscle
- **Common in:** Repeated external hip rotation under load — heel hooks, wide drop knees, stem moves
- **Presentation:** Deep buttock pain, possible referral down the back of the thigh (mimics sciatica), pain with prolonged sitting, worsens with aggressive hip external rotation positions

### Hip Impingement (Femoroacetabular Impingement — FAI)
- **Location:** Deep hip joint — where the femoral head meets the acetabulum
- **Types:** Cam (bony bump on femoral head), Pincer (overcoverage of acetabulum), or combined
- **Common in:** Climbers with anatomical variants; high step and rockover positions compress the joint
- **Presentation:** Deep groin pain with high steps and hip flexion past 90 degrees, clicking or catching sensation deep in the joint, stiffness after sitting, pain at end-range hip flexion with internal rotation
- **Note:** FAI is structural — requires imaging for diagnosis. Many people have FAI without symptoms; symptoms are driven by position and load.

### Hip Labral Irritation / Tear
- **Location:** The cartilaginous ring (labrum) around the hip socket
- **Common in:** Repeated loading in impingement positions, falls, twisting under load
- **Presentation:** Deep groin or lateral hip pain, clicking or catching in the hip, pain with prolonged walking or sitting, may feel unstable
- **Note:** Labral tears often accompany FAI and are diagnosed via MRI

### Adductor Strain
- **Location:** Inner thigh, adductor muscle group
- **Common in:** Wide bridging, stemming, splits-like moves on slab or chimney
- **Presentation:** Inner thigh pain with wide leg positions, weakness with resisted adduction, tenderness along the inner thigh

### Greater Trochanteric Pain Syndrome (Lateral Hip Pain)
- **Location:** Outer hip, over the bony prominence (greater trochanter)
- **Common in:** Compressive loading of the gluteal tendons — crossing legs, side sleeping, hip adduction positions
- **Presentation:** Lateral hip ache, tender to press on the outer hip, worse with lying on the affected side, sitting cross-legged, or climbing with repeated hip drop

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- Acute hip pain following a fall or significant force
- Pain that prevents weight bearing
- Locking or catching sensation that blocks movement
- Groin pain with significant swelling or bruising
- Hip pain in younger climbers that is progressively worsening (consider stress fracture)
- Numbness or tingling down the leg (possible nerve involvement)

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Avoid movements that reproduce sharp hip pain — high steps, wide stems, heel hooks in extreme hip positions
- Reduce climbing volume and intensity
- Gentle hip mobility work within pain-free range

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Gradually reintroduce hip flexion with controlled range
- Strengthen surrounding muscles: glutes (bridges, clamshells), hip flexors (controlled raises), adductors
- Avoid aggressive end-range positions until strength base is established

**Return to full climbing:**
- High steps and wide bridging return progressively — start with lower foot placements
- Flexibility and strength in the hip musculature reduces joint stress — consistent mobility work is beneficial
- Address any technique issues that overload the hip (e.g. dropping the hip excessively during high steps)

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Hip flexor strain (mild) | 2–4 weeks |
| Hip flexor strain (moderate) | 4–8 weeks |
| Piriformis syndrome | 4–10 weeks |
| FAI (conservative management) | 3–6 months |
| Labral tear (conservative) | 3–6 months |
| Adductor strain (mild) | 1–3 weeks |
| Greater trochanteric pain | 6–12 weeks |
