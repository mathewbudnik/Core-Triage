# Lower Back Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Lower back pain is common in climbers, particularly those who train heavily on overhanging terrain, boards, and steep routes. The repeated spinal flexion and extension under load, combined with the sustained tension required for core engagement, places significant demand on the lumbar spine and surrounding structures. Most climbing-related back pain is non-specific and responds well to load modification and movement.

---

## Common Injury Patterns

### Non-Specific Lower Back Pain
- **Location:** Lumbar region, often diffuse
- **Common in:** High-volume training, sustained overhanging climbing, sudden load increases
- **Presentation:** Dull aching in the lower back, stiffness especially in the morning, pain that eases with movement and worsens with prolonged sitting or standing, may refer into the glutes
- **Note:** This is the most common category — most acute lower back pain resolves within 4–6 weeks with appropriate management

### Lumbar Muscle / Facet Strain
- **Location:** Paraspinal muscles and facet joints, lumbar spine
- **Common in:** Awkward body positions under load, sudden dynamic moves, volume spikes
- **Presentation:** Localised back pain, muscle spasm, pain with certain movements, difficulty finding a comfortable position acutely

### Lumbar Disc Irritation
- **Location:** Intervertebral discs, most commonly L4/L5 or L5/S1
- **Common in:** Repeated loaded spinal flexion — overhanging climbing, board moves requiring significant hip flexion, approach hiking with heavy packs
- **Presentation:** Central or unilateral lower back pain, possible referral into the buttock and thigh, stiffness after rest, pain with forward bending
- **Note:** Disc issues exist on a spectrum — many people have disc changes on imaging without symptoms. Symptoms are what matter, not imaging findings alone.

### Radiculopathy (Nerve Root Irritation)
- **Location:** Lumbar nerve roots — symptoms travel down the leg
- **Common in:** Disc herniation compressing a nerve root; can follow acute injury or develop gradually
- **Presentation:** Pain, tingling, or numbness that travels from the lower back down into the buttock, thigh, calf, or foot (sciatica pattern); may be accompanied by weakness; symptoms worse with sitting and forward flexion

### Spondylolysis / Spondylolisthesis
- **Location:** Pars interarticularis (a small bone bridge in the vertebra)
- **Common in:** Young climbers, repetitive spinal extension under load (overhanging climbing, mantling)
- **Presentation:** Unilateral or central lower back pain, worse with back extension and rotation, often insidious onset in adolescent climbers
- **Note:** Requires imaging for diagnosis. Important to identify in young climbers — requires structured management.

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation Promptly

- Bowel or bladder dysfunction (urgency, incontinence, retention) — **seek emergency evaluation**
- Saddle anaesthesia (numbness in the groin/inner thigh region) — **seek emergency evaluation**
- Progressive leg weakness
- Pain following significant trauma (fall from height)
- Night pain that consistently wakes you from sleep
- Unexplained weight loss with back pain
- Back pain in a climber under 20 with no improvement — consider spondylolysis

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Reduce climbing volume — avoid steep overhanging terrain
- Keep moving — prolonged bed rest is not recommended for most back pain
- Find comfortable positions; avoid sustained postures that aggravate symptoms
- Gentle walking and easy movement generally helps

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Gradual return to climbing — start with vertical and slab before overhanging
- Core stability work: focus on controlled activation (dead bugs, bird dogs) rather than high-load exercises early
- Hip flexibility is closely linked to lower back load in climbing — address hip flexor and hamstring tightness
- Avoid campus training and explosive moves until pain-free

**Return to full climbing:**
- Overhanging and board climbing return progressively — assess response session by session
- Address technique: excessive lumbar hyperextension on overhangs, poor hip engagement on steep terrain, and inefficient flag positions can all increase spinal load
- Regular core and hip strengthening as maintenance — not just during injury

**Lifestyle factors:**
- Prolonged sitting before climbing sessions increases back stiffness — brief movement before climbing helps
- Approach hike pack weight: reduce pack load when back is symptomatic
- Sleep position: side-lying with a pillow between knees often reduces overnight discomfort

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Acute non-specific back pain | 2–6 weeks |
| Muscle / facet strain | 2–4 weeks |
| Disc irritation (mild) | 4–8 weeks |
| Disc herniation with radiculopathy | 6–12 weeks (most resolve conservatively) |
| Spondylolysis (managed) | 3–6 months |
