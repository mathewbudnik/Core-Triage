# Lower Back Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Lower back pain is common in climbers training heavily on overhanging terrain and boards. Repeated spinal flexion-extension under load and sustained core tension place significant demand on the lumbar spine. Most acute climbing-related back pain is non-specific and resolves with load reduction, but serious pathology must be screened.

**Disclaimer:** This information is for triage guidance only and does not constitute medical advice or diagnosis. Seek professional medical evaluation for any injury. In case of emergency call 911.

---

## EMERGENCY — Cauda Equina Syndrome

**This is a medical emergency. Call 911 or go to the ER immediately if any of the following are present:**
- Bladder or bowel dysfunction: urinary retention, incontinence, or sudden change in bladder/bowel control
- Saddle anaesthesia: numbness in the groin, inner thighs, or genitals
- Bilateral leg weakness or numbness
- Progressive lower limb weakness

Cauda equina syndrome is caused by massive disc herniation compressing the spinal cord bundle. Delayed treatment causes permanent paralysis and incontinence. Do not wait.

## Lumbar Strain

**Clinical name:** Lumbar musculoligamentous strain
**Mechanism:** Stemming in wide positions, mantling, roof climbing with sustained spinal extension, sudden awkward movement under load
**Location:** Diffuse lower back, paraspinal muscles

**Symptoms:**
- Diffuse lower back pain and muscle spasm
- Pain with movement, stiffness especially in the morning
- Eases with gentle movement, worsens with prolonged postures
- No leg symptoms (key differentiator from disc/nerve involvement)

**Recovery:** Acute strain: 2–4 weeks. Recurrent or chronic: requires core rehabilitation and movement retraining.

**Rehab — McGill Big 3:**
- Modified curl-up: small spinal curl with one knee bent
- Side plank (modified as needed)
- Bird dog: opposite arm/leg extension from all-fours
- Dead bug progressions follow once Big 3 is pain-free
- Hip hinge retraining for approach hiking and start of routes
- Avoid sustained lumbar flexion positions when acute

**Severity:** Mild to moderate. Severe (pain >7/10, unable to move): evaluation within 48 hours.
**Can continue climbing:** Modified — avoid steep overhanging terrain; vertical and slab only
**Hangboard clearance:** Avoid when acute; resume when pain-free with standing
**Campus board clearance:** Last to return; avoid sustained lumbar flexion under load

## Lumbar Disc Herniation

**Clinical name:** Lumbar intervertebral disc herniation, with or without radiculopathy
**Mechanism:** Sustained loaded spinal flexion in roof climbing, repeated compression-flexion from overhanging terrain, sudden loading event

**Symptoms:**
- Central or unilateral lower back pain
- Radicular symptoms following dermatomal pattern:
  - L4 nerve: inner calf, medial foot
  - L5 nerve: outer calf, top of foot, big toe
  - S1 nerve: heel, outer foot, little toe
- Pain worse with sitting and forward bending
- Pain with coughing, sneezing, or straining (Valsalva manoeuvre)
- Stiffness after prolonged rest

**Key differentiation:** Radiculopathy (leg pain following dermatomal pattern) strongly suggests disc herniation with nerve compression. Referred pain into the glutes without a dermatomal pattern is more likely muscular.

**Red flags (Cauda Equina):** Saddle anaesthesia, bilateral leg symptoms, bladder/bowel dysfunction = 911 immediately
**Recovery:** 80% resolve conservatively in 6–12 weeks. Persistent radiculopathy may require epidural steroid injection or surgical decompression.

**Climbing modifications:** Avoid sustained flexion positions; lumbar support during long approaches; avoid roof climbing until fully pain-free.

**Severity:** Without leg symptoms: moderate. With unilateral radiculopathy: severe — see specialist within 1 week. With bilateral symptoms or cauda equina signs: emergency.
**Can continue climbing:** Modified vertical/slab only if pain <3/10; no overhanging terrain
**Campus board clearance:** 3–4 months minimum after full symptom resolution

## Spondylolysis / Spondylolisthesis

**Location:** Pars interarticularis — a small bony bridge in the vertebra
**Common in:** Young climbers; repetitive spinal extension under load (overhanging climbing, mantling)

**Symptoms:** Unilateral or central lower back pain, worse with back extension and rotation, often insidious onset in adolescent climbers. Spondylolisthesis = forward slippage of one vertebra over another.

**Requires imaging for diagnosis.** Important to identify in young climbers — requires structured management.
**Recovery:** 3–6 months. Sports physiotherapy and load modification essential.
**Severity:** Moderate to severe — requires imaging and specialist guidance before returning to overhanging climbing

## Non-Specific Lower Back Pain

**The most common category.** Most acute lower back pain in climbers resolves within 4–6 weeks with appropriate management.

**Location:** Lumbar region, often diffuse
**Common in:** High training volume, sustained overhanging climbing, sudden load increases, sessions with heavy pack approach
**Symptoms:** Dull aching, stiffness especially in the morning, eases with movement, may refer into the glutes but not below the knee
**Recovery:** 2–6 weeks

---

## Lumbar Facet Strain

**Location:** Facet joints and paraspinal muscles
**Mechanism:** Awkward body positions under load, sudden dynamic moves, volume spikes
**Symptoms:** Localized back pain, muscle spasm, difficulty finding a comfortable position acutely, pain with rotation
**Recovery:** 2–4 weeks

---

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation (All Lower Back Injuries)

- **EMERGENCY:** Bowel or bladder dysfunction, saddle anaesthesia, bilateral leg weakness — call 911
- Progressive leg weakness or worsening neurological deficit
- Pain after significant trauma (fall from height)
- Night pain that consistently wakes from sleep
- Unexplained weight loss with back pain
- Back pain in a climber under 20 with no improvement — consider spondylolysis
- Radicular symptoms below the knee

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Reduce climbing — avoid all overhanging terrain
- Keep moving — prolonged bed rest is harmful for most back pain
- Gentle walking and easy movement generally helps more than rest
- Comfortable positions: avoid sustained postures that aggravate symptoms

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Start with vertical and slab climbing before overhanging
- Core stability: McGill Big 3 → dead bug → hip hinge retraining
- Address hip flexibility: tight hip flexors and hamstrings increase lumbar load in climbing
- Avoid campus training and explosive moves until pain-free

**Return to full climbing:**
- Overhanging and board climbing return progressively
- Technique check: excessive lumbar hyperextension on overhangs and poor hip engagement increase spinal load
- Regular core and hip strengthening as maintenance

**Lifestyle factors:**
- Brief movement before sessions reduces stiffness
- Reduce pack weight on approach when back is symptomatic
- Side-lying with a pillow between knees often reduces overnight discomfort

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Acute non-specific back pain | 2–6 weeks |
| Lumbar muscle/facet strain | 2–4 weeks |
| Disc irritation (mild) | 4–8 weeks |
| Disc herniation with radiculopathy | 6–12 weeks (most resolve conservatively) |
| Spondylolysis (managed) | 3–6 months |
