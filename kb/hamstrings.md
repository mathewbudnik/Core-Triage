# Hamstring Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Hamstring injuries in climbing have one dominant cause: heel hooking. The hamstrings are the primary movers when you actively pull yourself toward the wall using a heel hook — and the harder the pull, the more they're loaded, often in positions of significant hip flexion that put them at end-range while loaded.

The most common climbing hamstring injury is **proximal hamstring tendinopathy** at the sit-bone, but acute mid-belly strains and biceps femoris (outer hamstring) injuries are also common, especially during a single high-effort heel hook attempt. These injuries are frustrating because they're slow to heal and easy to re-aggravate. `needs_review`

## Common Injury Patterns

### Proximal Hamstring Tendinopathy (Sit-Bone Pain)
- **Location:** Sit-bone (ischial tuberosity), where the hamstrings attach to the pelvis
- **Common in:** Heel hooking, repeated high-tension heel pulls, prolonged sitting on hard surfaces
- **Presentation:** Deep ache at the sit-bone, worse with prolonged sitting (driving, belaying, working at a desk) and with heel hook pulling. May refer down the back of the thigh. Often gradual onset.
- **Note:** This is the classic climbing hamstring injury. Slow to heal — 3–6 months of consistent loading is normal. The temptation to skip rehab and climb through it is the main reason it persists. `needs_review`

### Biceps Femoris (Outer Hamstring) Strain
- **Location:** Outer back of the thigh, often near the knee or in the muscle belly
- **Common in:** Heavy single heel hook efforts, especially when the foot slips or the move requires maximal pulling
- **Presentation:** Sudden sharp pain during a heel hook, often with a felt pull. Tender to press on the outer hamstring. Pain with knee flexion against resistance and with hip extension.
- **Note:** Biceps femoris is the most loaded hamstring during a heel hook because of the external rotation component — this is why heel hook injuries cluster on the outer side. `needs_review`

### Hamstring Strain — Mid-Belly
- **Location:** Mid back of the thigh
- **Common in:** Sudden eccentric loads — a foot cut that turns into a hang, an unexpected swing, sprinting on the approach
- **Presentation:** Sharp pain at the moment of injury, sometimes with bruising in days following. Walking is uncomfortable, especially on stairs. Resisted knee flexion reproduces the symptom.
- **Note:** Less common in climbing than proximal tendinopathy, but happens. Recovery follows standard muscle-strain timelines. `needs_review`

### High Hamstring Tendinopathy (Variant)
- **Location:** Just below the sit-bone, where the hamstring tendon transitions to muscle
- **Common in:** Same mechanisms as proximal tendinopathy, often in climbers who also sit a lot
- **Presentation:** Similar to proximal tendinopathy but the tender point is slightly lower. Often presents with sit-bone bursitis-like pain during sitting.
- **Note:** Treated similarly to proximal tendinopathy — progressive loading is the answer. `needs_review`

### Sciatic Nerve Irritation (Sciatica) — Differential
- **Location:** Pain that travels down the leg from the buttock or back, sometimes mimicking hamstring pain
- **Common in:** Climbers with disc-related issues or piriformis syndrome (see glutes.md)
- **Presentation:** Burning, electric, or shooting pain down the leg, often crossing knee level. May be accompanied by numbness or tingling. Worse with positions that load the lumbar spine or stretch the sciatic nerve.
- **Note:** Important differential — true hamstring pain stays in the muscle. Symptoms that travel below the knee or change with back position are usually nerve, not muscle. `needs_review`

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- Sudden severe pain with a felt pop and significant immediate bruising — possible high-grade tear or proximal hamstring avulsion
- Inability to walk normally (limping severely) more than 24 hours after injury
- Numbness or tingling in the leg (suggests nerve involvement, not muscle)
- Sit-bone pain with significant unexplained weight loss
- A defect or asymmetry in the back-of-thigh muscle bulk

Proximal hamstring avulsion (where the tendon pulls off the bone) is uncommon but a surgical consideration if it happens — sudden severe pain with significant bruising and weakness warrants imaging.

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- No heel hooks at all
- No heavy hip hinges, deadlifts, or sprinting
- Avoid prolonged sitting on hard surfaces (use a cushion, stand often)
- Easy walking is fine and encouraged
- Isometric hamstring work once acute pain settles: long-lever bridges held for 30s × 5 reps, daily

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Progress to slow eccentric hamstring work (Nordics, Romanian deadlifts at low weight)
- Reintroduce easy heel hooks on large comfortable holds at very low intensity
- Continue isometric loading; tendons need time under tension to remodel
- Pain during exercise up to 3/10 is acceptable for tendinopathy — don't push past that

**Return to full climbing:**
- Heel hooks return in stages: comfortable hold → harder hold → progressively more pull
- Expect a flare or two during reload — manage with the 24-hour rule, don't stop entirely
- Strength differences side-to-side need to be addressed before returning to maximum efforts
- Most climbers benefit from making heavy hamstring loading a permanent part of training (Nordics, RDLs)

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Hamstring strain — grade I (mild) | 1–3 weeks |
| Hamstring strain — grade II (moderate) | 4–8 weeks |
| Hamstring strain — grade III (severe) | 3–6 months, possible surgical consult |
| Biceps femoris strain (mild) | 2–4 weeks |
| Proximal hamstring tendinopathy | 3–6 months of consistent loading (often longer) |
| High hamstring tendinopathy | 3–6 months |
| Proximal hamstring avulsion (rupture) | Surgical consult — recovery 6–9 months |
