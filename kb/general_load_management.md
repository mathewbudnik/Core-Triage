# General Climbing Load Management

## Overview

Most climbing injuries are overuse injuries — meaning they develop from training loads that exceed the body's capacity to recover. Understanding load management is the single most effective tool for both preventing and recovering from climbing injuries. This applies to fingers, elbows, shoulders, and all other tissues.

---

## The Core Principle

Your tissues adapt to load over time — but adaptation takes longer than fitness gains. You can get fitter faster than your tendons can strengthen. This gap is where most injuries happen.

> "The injury usually isn't from the session that caused it — it's from the weeks of accumulated load that preceded it."

---

## Pain Monitoring During Activity

Use the 0–10 pain scale as your guide during training:

| Pain Level | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 0–3 | Mild, acceptable | Continue with monitoring |
| 4–5 | Moderate | Reduce load, modify movement |
| 6+ | High | Stop the aggravating activity |
| Sharp/electric | Nerve or acute tissue | Stop immediately |

The goal during recovery is to keep pain at or below 3/10 during training. Pain above 3/10 signals you're exceeding tissue capacity.

**The 24-hour rule:** If pain is significantly elevated more than 24 hours after a session, that session was too much. Use this as feedback to reduce load in the next session.

---

## Common Load Errors That Cause Injury

- **Volume spike:** Dramatically increasing training days or session length (e.g. starting a new board membership and climbing 5x/week immediately)
- **Intensity spike:** Jumping to max hangs, limit bouldering, or competition prep without building base fitness
- **Too little rest:** Not leaving enough days between hard sessions for tissue recovery
- **Returning too soon:** Coming back from rest or injury at previous levels instead of building back gradually
- **Ignoring early warning signs:** Pushing through persistent low-grade pain until it becomes a full injury

---

## The One Variable Rule

When building back from injury or adding load, change only **one variable at a time**:

- **Volume** (how much — total time, number of routes/problems)
- **Intensity** (how hard — grade, hold size, body tension demands)
- **Frequency** (how often — training days per week)

Increase one, hold the others steady. Wait 1–2 weeks to assess response before changing another variable.

---

## Recovery Factors That Directly Affect Healing

**Sleep:** Tissue repair happens primarily during sleep. Less than 7–8 hours consistently slows tendon and ligament recovery.

**Nutrition:** Adequate protein (1.6–2.2g/kg body weight) supports tissue repair. Collagen synthesis (relevant for tendons and pulleys) is supported by vitamin C.

**Stress:** Psychological stress increases systemic inflammation and delays recovery. High-stress periods require more conservative training loads.

**Hydration:** Tendons and cartilage rely on hydration for mechanical properties. Chronic dehydration impairs tissue health.

**Age:** Recovery times lengthen with age. Climbers over 35 generally need more rest between hard sessions.

---

## Returning to Climbing After Injury

**General progression:**
1. **Pain-free daily life** — before starting any climbing
2. **Easy climbing, open-hand grips, large holds** — assess response
3. **Moderate climbing, mixed grip styles** — increase volume gradually
4. **Harder climbing, sport-specific grips** — increase intensity gradually
5. **Full training, limit moves, max efforts** — last to return

At each stage: if pain spikes above 3/10 or persists 24+ hours after, step back one level.

**Never return at pre-injury training loads.** Always start at 50–60% of previous volume and intensity and build from there.

---

## Antagonist Training

Climbing is heavily pulling-dominant. Imbalances between pulling muscles (lats, biceps, finger flexors) and pushing muscles (chest, triceps, shoulder external rotators, finger extensors) contribute to overuse injuries.

Include in your weekly routine:
- Push-ups or pressing movements
- Shoulder external rotation exercises
- Wrist and finger extensor work (rubber band extensions)
- Scapular stabilisation (rows, face pulls)

---

## When Conservative Management Isn't Enough

Seek professional evaluation if:
- Pain is not improving after 2–3 weeks of genuine load reduction
- Pain is present at rest or waking you from sleep
- There is significant swelling, bruising, or deformity
- You have neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling, weakness)
- You are unsure what structure is injured
- The injury followed significant trauma
