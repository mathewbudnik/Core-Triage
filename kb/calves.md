# Calf Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Calf injuries in climbers come from two main sources: aggressive smearing on slab (which loads the calves under high tension at full extension) and the long approach hikes that bookend most climbing days. Multi-pitch routes and bouldering trips that pile on volume are common precipitating factors.

The calf is two muscles working together: gastrocnemius (the larger, more visible upper calf) and soleus (the deeper, lower calf). They have slightly different injury patterns. Achilles tendon injuries — which sit at the bottom of the calf — are covered in `ankle_foot.md`. `needs_review`

## Common Injury Patterns

### Gastrocnemius Strain ("Calf Strain")
- **Location:** Upper calf, often medial (inner) head of the gastroc
- **Common in:** Sudden forceful push-off — sprinting on the approach, an aggressive smear, jumping off a boulder
- **Presentation:** Sudden sharp pain in the upper calf, often with a felt "pop" or "kick from behind." Walking is uncomfortable, especially uphill or pushing off the toes. Tender to press on the muscle belly. Bruising may appear in the days following.
- **Note:** The medial gastroc is the most commonly injured. Often called "tennis leg" but it's just as common in climbers. `needs_review`

### Soleus Strain
- **Location:** Lower calf, deep to the gastroc
- **Common in:** Chronic loading — long approach hikes, extended smearing on slab, multi-pitch days
- **Presentation:** Deep, lower calf ache. More gradual onset than gastroc strain. Worse with prolonged walking and standing.
- **Note:** Soleus injuries take longer to heal than gastroc injuries because the soleus has slower-twitch fibers and a thicker tendon — it remodels slowly. `needs_review`

### Plantaris Rupture
- **Location:** Behind the knee or upper calf, on the inner side
- **Common in:** Same mechanisms as gastroc strain — a forceful push-off
- **Presentation:** Sudden snap behind the knee, often with significant pain and bruising over the next 24–48 hours. Mimics Achilles rupture in feel — but unlike Achilles, push-off strength is preserved.
- **Note:** The plantaris is a small, vestigial muscle. Its rupture is benign and resolves on its own with no specific treatment beyond load management. The main reason to know about it is differential — climbers convinced they ruptured their Achilles often have a plantaris tear. `needs_review`

### Calf Cramping / Overuse
- **Location:** Diffuse calf soreness or cramping, often bilateral
- **Common in:** Sudden volume increases — long climbing trip, multi-pitch days, returning from a layoff
- **Presentation:** Generalized calf fatigue, soreness, or cramping during or after activity. No specific tender point. Resolves with rest and hydration.
- **Note:** This is overuse, not injury. Address volume and recovery, not rehab. `needs_review`

### Achilles Tendinopathy / Rupture
- **Location:** Lower calf, at or just above the heel
- **See:** `ankle_foot.md` for full coverage. Achilles issues commonly present as "lower calf pain" and should be considered when evaluating any climber's calf complaint, especially after long approach days.

### Posterior Compartment Syndrome (Rare)
- **Location:** Deep in the calf, often bilateral
- **Common in:** Climbers who do significant running or hiking volume
- **Presentation:** Tightness, ache, or pressure in the calf during activity that resolves with rest. May progress to numbness or weakness in the foot during sustained activity.
- **Note:** Rare but worth knowing — chronic exertional compartment syndrome requires specialist evaluation. `needs_review`

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- Inability to push up onto the toes after a snap at the back of the ankle (possible Achilles rupture — see `ankle_foot.md`)
- Significant calf swelling that is hot, red, or asymmetric — possible deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and warrants prompt evaluation
- Calf pain after a long flight, period of immobility, or significant trauma
- Numbness or tingling in the foot accompanying calf pain
- Pain that wakes you at night and is unrelieved by position change
- Severe pain with inability to bear weight more than 24 hours after injury

DVT is the one calf complaint that can become a true emergency (pulmonary embolism). Climbers are mostly low-risk, but post-trip flights, long drives, or recent immobilization shift the picture — flag any swollen, hot, asymmetric calf for prompt evaluation.

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Reduce climbing volume; drop slab and aggressive smearing
- No long approach hikes; drive to the crag if possible
- Walking is fine; avoid uphill walking with the painful leg
- Heel lifts (a folded sock in the heel of both shoes) reduce calf load
- Gentle isometric calf raises in pain-free range, daily

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Progress to slow eccentric calf raises (3 × 10, on stairs, both heels down slowly)
- Reintroduce moderate hiking on flat terrain with a light pack
- Vertical climbing on solid footholds is fine; minimal smearing
- Address calf flexibility — a foam roller and daily stretching shorten recovery time

**Return to full climbing:**
- Walk → hike → smear → multi-pitch is the sensible reload order
- Drop pack weight on first approach hikes back; build up over weeks
- Climbing-trip prep: in the weeks before a long trip, build calf endurance with progressively longer hikes
- Calf strains often recur — finish rehab fully before returning to long days

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Gastrocnemius strain — grade I (mild) | 2–4 weeks |
| Gastrocnemius strain — grade II (moderate) | 4–8 weeks |
| Gastrocnemius strain — grade III (severe) | 3–6 months, possible surgical consult |
| Soleus strain | 4–10 weeks (slower than gastroc) |
| Plantaris rupture | 4–8 weeks (resolves spontaneously) |
| Calf cramping / overuse | 1–2 weeks of reduced volume |
| Posterior compartment syndrome | Specialist evaluation; conservative or surgical depending on severity |
