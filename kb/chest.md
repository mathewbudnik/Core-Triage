# Chest, Pec, and Rib Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Chest and rib complaints in climbers are less common than shoulder or finger issues but tend to be more alarming when they happen — partly because of overlap with cardiopulmonary symptoms, partly because they affect breathing under load. The most common patterns are pec major strain (often from compression climbing or a hard dynamic catch), chronic pec minor tightness driving forward-shoulder posture, and rib-area irritation from intense pulling, twisting, or sustained breath-holding.

Most chest-region complaints are muscular and resolve with load management plus a deliberate antagonist (mid-back) plan. The exception is any presentation with breathing changes or severe disproportionate pain — these warrant immediate medical evaluation to rule out cardiopulmonary causes before any musculoskeletal diagnosis. `needs_review`

## Common Injury Patterns

### Pectoralis Major Strain
- **Location:** Anterior chest wall — most commonly the lower edge near the sternal head or at the humeral insertion (front of the armpit)
- **Common in:** Compression climbing (squeezing pinches and slopers together), hard dynamic catches with the arm extended, full-tension overhang moves
- **Presentation:** Sharp pain felt during the offending move, sometimes with an audible pop. Bruising can appear over the next 24–72 hours along the lower pec or front of the arm. Worse with active arm adduction (squeezing inward) and stretching the arm overhead/back.
- **Note:** A complete tear of the pec major (especially at the humeral insertion) is a surgical injury — visible deformity, sudden pop, significant weakness, and bruising tracking down the arm are signs requiring urgent evaluation. `needs_review`

### Pec Minor Tightness / Overactivity
- **Location:** Deep to the upper-outer pec major, just below the collarbone
- **Common in:** Long-time climbers with chronic forward-shoulder posture from pulling, hangboarding, and desk work
- **Presentation:** Tight, ropey pec minor that's tender to press. Drives forward-rolled shoulder position, restricted overhead reach, and downstream shoulder impingement. Pain may radiate into the front of the arm or up toward the collarbone.
- **Note:** Pec minor passes over the brachial plexus — severe tightness occasionally causes neurogenic symptoms (numbness, pulse changes in the arm). If those are present, escalate. `needs_review`

### Costochondritis
- **Location:** Front of the chest at the rib-sternum junction (costochondral joints), often more on one side
- **Common in:** High-volume pulling phases, hangboard cycles, sudden training spikes
- **Presentation:** Sharp localized pain at the rib-sternum junction that's reproducible with direct pressure. Worse with deep breaths, coughing, and twisting. Often misinterpreted as cardiac pain — but reproducible tenderness makes it clearly musculoskeletal.
- **Note:** Self-limiting in most cases. Reduce load, address breathing pattern. Persistent or worsening symptoms warrant evaluation. `needs_review`

### Rib Stress Fracture (Uncommon)
- **Location:** Lateral or posterior ribs (often the lower ribs along the serratus or external oblique attachments)
- **Common in:** Very high-volume periods, heavy crimping sessions where the climber bears down hard, intense board climbing
- **Presentation:** Aching that builds over a session and persists for days. Often very point-tender on a single rib. Painful with deep breath, sneezing, and trunk rotation. May develop after a sustained training block.
- **Note:** Imaging (bone scan or MRI) is more reliable than X-ray for stress fractures. Requires 4–8 weeks off the offending pattern. `needs_review`

### Serratus Anterior Strain
- **Location:** Side of the chest wall (axillary line), under and behind the lower armpit
- **Common in:** Extreme overhead reaching, repeated punch-throughs, throwing-style dyno moves
- **Presentation:** Sharp or pulling pain along the lateral chest wall during reaching or pressing moves. Tender along the rib attachments. Often misidentified as a lat or oblique injury.
- **Note:** Serratus is critical for scapular protraction and upward rotation — strain here often co-presents with scapular dyskinesis. Rehab needs both rest and scapular control work. `needs_review`

### Costovertebral / Rib Joint Dysfunction
- **Location:** Posterior chest, following a rib line from spine to side
- **Common in:** Gastons, deep drop knees, twisting moves under load
- **Presentation:** Sharp localized pain along a rib that's worse with deep breath, twisting, or sustained postures. Often presents as "I can't take a deep breath without it catching." Pain is usually one-sided.
- **Note:** Often resolves with thoracic mobility work and gentle rotation. Persistent symptoms benefit from manual therapy. `needs_review`

## Red Flags — Seek Immediate Evaluation

- **Severe chest pain with any breathing change** — shortness of breath, painful inspiration, or unable to take a deep breath. Always rule out cardiac or pulmonary causes first.
- **Pain radiating into the jaw, left arm, or back** with sweating, nausea, or shortness of breath — emergency cardiac evaluation
- **Sudden severe chest pain after a fall** with visible deformity, instability of the chest wall, or breathing difficulty
- **Complete pec major rupture signs** — sudden pop with significant weakness, visible defect or deformity in the pec, and bruising tracking down the arm
- **Numbness, tingling, or pulse changes in the arm** that accompany chest or pec tightness — possible neurovascular compression
- **Severe persistent pain unrelieved by rest or position change**, especially if it wakes you at night

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Reduce climbing volume; eliminate compression problems, hard dynamic catches, and full-overhang sessions
- Gentle pain-free overhead reaching and chest opening
- Slow diaphragmatic breathing (climbers chronically breath-hold under load — this drives upper-chest tension)
- Avoid forced stretching of the pec until pain settles

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Daily pec minor self-release with a lacrosse ball against a wall
- Open-book rotations and thoracic foam roller extension to address mobility
- Band pull-aparts and face pulls — climbers need posterior chain volume to balance pec dominance
- Reintroduce light pressing (push-up plus, light cable fly) once pain-free at rest

**Return to full climbing:**
- Gradual reload — start with vertical climbing, then add overhang, then compression
- Maintain antagonist (mid-back, posterior shoulder) work indefinitely
- Address breathing pattern under load — exhale through hard moves rather than breath-holding

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Pec major strain (mild–moderate) | 2–6 weeks |
| Pec major complete tear / rupture | Surgical — 4–6 months |
| Pec minor tightness | Ongoing — addressed with daily maintenance |
| Costochondritis | 2–8 weeks |
| Rib stress fracture | 6–12 weeks off offending pattern |
| Serratus anterior strain | 3–6 weeks |
| Costovertebral dysfunction | 2–4 weeks with mobility work |

## When to Escalate

Chest and rib region complaints are unique among climbing injuries because of the cardiopulmonary overlap. Default to caution: any severe, sudden, or breathing-affecting chest symptom warrants medical evaluation before assuming a musculoskeletal cause. The cost of unnecessary evaluation is small; the cost of missing a cardiac, pulmonary, or vascular event can be catastrophic. `needs_review`
