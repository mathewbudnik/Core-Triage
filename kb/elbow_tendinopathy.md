# Elbow Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Elbow injuries are the second most common injury site in climbers after fingers. The majority are overuse-related tendinopathies driven by repetitive high-load pulling. They tend to develop gradually and become chronic if not addressed early. Nerve-related issues are also common given the anatomy of the elbow.

---

## Common Injury Patterns

### Medial Elbow Tendinopathy ("Climber's Elbow" / Golfer's Elbow)
- **Location:** Inner (medial) side of the elbow, at the common flexor tendon origin on the medial epicondyle
- **Common in:** High-volume pulling, steep bouldering, board climbing, campusing, crimping — medial is far more common than lateral in climbers
- **Presentation:** Pain on the inner elbow, worse with gripping and wrist flexion; may radiate down the forearm; stiffness first thing in the morning; tenderness directly on the medial epicondyle

### Lateral Elbow Tendinopathy ("Tennis Elbow")
- **Location:** Outer (lateral) side of the elbow, at the common extensor tendon
- **Common in:** Less common in climbers; often from overuse of wrist extensors, backstepping, or antagonist training imbalances
- **Presentation:** Pain on the outer elbow, worse with gripping and wrist extension; tenderness on the lateral epicondyle

### Distal Biceps Tendinopathy / Strain
- **Location:** Front of the elbow, at the biceps tendon insertion
- **Common in:** Explosive pulling, dynamic catches, hard lock-offs
- **Presentation:** Deep anterior elbow pain, weakness with supination (turning palm up), tenderness at the insertion point
- **Red flag:** Complete distal biceps rupture presents as sudden severe pain, possible "pop," visible bruising, and a Popeye deformity (bunched muscle belly)

### Ulnar Nerve Irritation (Cubital Tunnel Syndrome)
- **Location:** Medial elbow, where the ulnar nerve passes through the cubital tunnel
- **Common in:** Repeated elbow flexion under load; also from resting elbows on surfaces
- **Presentation:** Tingling or numbness in the ring and little fingers, inner forearm aching, possible weakness in grip and pinch; symptoms often worse at night or after prolonged elbow flexion

### Posterior Impingement
- **Location:** Back of the elbow, olecranon region
- **Common in:** Repeated full elbow extension under load (e.g. mantling, deadhanging)
- **Presentation:** Pain at the back of the elbow at end-range extension; sometimes a clicking sensation

---

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation

- Sudden pop or snap in the elbow with immediate weakness or deformity (possible biceps rupture)
- Tingling, numbness, or weakness in the hand or fingers
- Significant swelling after acute injury
- Night pain that disturbs sleep consistently
- Symptoms not improving after 4–6 weeks of load reduction

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Reduce climbing volume significantly — avoid steep terrain, campusing, and max-intensity sessions
- Avoid moves that reproduce sharp elbow pain
- Keep pain below 3/10 during any activity
- Gentle active range of motion — avoid forced stretching into pain

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Isometric loading is typically well tolerated early — sustained holds at moderate intensity, pain-free
- Gradually reintroduce pulling volume on moderate terrain
- Address training load spikes — tendinopathy is almost always driven by doing too much too soon
- Antagonist training (wrist extension, push-ups, shoulder press) helps restore balance

**Return to full training:**
- Reintroduce board sessions and max-intensity pulling only when pain-free through normal climbing for 2+ weeks
- Campusing and limit bouldering are the last to return
- Eccentric loading programs (under physiotherapist guidance) have strong evidence for chronic tendinopathy

**Load factors to investigate:**
- Did volume spike recently? (new board, new training cycle, competition prep)
- Is sleep and recovery adequate?
- Are antagonist muscles (pushing muscles) significantly weaker than pulling muscles?

## Typical Recovery Timelines (Educational Estimates)

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Mild tendinopathy | 4–8 weeks |
| Moderate tendinopathy | 2–4 months |
| Chronic tendinopathy (>3 months) | 4–6+ months with structured rehab |
| Distal biceps strain | 6–12 weeks |
| Distal biceps rupture | Surgery + 4–6 months |
