# Elbow Injuries in Climbing

## Overview

Elbow injuries are the second most common injury site in climbers after fingers. The majority are overuse tendinopathies driven by high-load pulling. They develop gradually but become chronic if ignored — the most common mistake is continuing to train through medial elbow pain, converting a 6-week recovery into a 12-month one.

**Disclaimer:** This information is for triage guidance only and does not constitute medical advice or diagnosis. Seek professional medical evaluation for any injury. In case of emergency call 911.

---

## Medial Epicondylitis — "Climber's Elbow"

**Clinical name:** Medial epicondylosis, common flexor-pronator tendinopathy
**Mechanism:** Overuse of wrist flexors and pronators from sustained crimping, high training volume, insufficient recovery. Most common in intermediate to advanced climbers who train heavily on the hangboard.
**Location:** Inner (medial) side of the elbow

**Symptoms:**
- Pain at the medial epicondyle (inside of elbow)
- Pain with wrist flexion under load
- Pain with forearm pronation
- Morning stiffness
- Tenderness on direct palpation of the medial epicondyle
- May radiate down the forearm

**Critical warning:** Climbers who ignore medial elbow pain and continue training convert acute tendinitis to chronic tendinosis — extending recovery from weeks to 12+ months. Pain below 3/10 during climbing does not mean the tendon is tolerating load.

**Red flags:**
- Ulnar nerve symptoms (tingling in ring/pinky fingers) — may indicate cubital tunnel involvement
- Elbow instability
- Significant pain at rest

**Rehab phases:**
- **Acute:** Active rest (easy climbing if pain <3/10), ice, short-term NSAIDs if appropriate, avoid all painful movements
- **Subacute:** Eccentric wrist flexion exercises, pronation/supination with progressive load, soft tissue work at common flexor origin
- **Return to sport:** Progressive hangboard starting with open-hand; no campus board for 12 weeks; monitor pain response after every session

**Recovery:** 6–16 weeks. Frequently underestimated.
**Severity:** Mild: modify and monitor. Moderate: physio within 1 week. Severe (pain >7/10 or significant weakness): evaluation within 48 hours.
**Can continue climbing:** Modified — avoid steep terrain, campusing, crimping, max-intensity sessions
**Hangboard clearance:** Minimum 4 weeks from symptom onset; start with open-hand at reduced load
**Campus board clearance:** 12 weeks minimum from symptom resolution; last activity to return

## Lateral Epicondylitis — "Tennis Elbow"

**Clinical name:** Lateral epicondylosis, common extensor tendinopathy (ECRB most common)
**Mechanism:** Overuse of wrist extensors — compression climbing (stemming), underclings, overhanging climbing where extensors stabilize, antagonist training imbalance
**Location:** Outer (lateral) side of the elbow

**Symptoms:**
- Pain at the lateral epicondyle (outside of elbow)
- Pain with wrist extension under load
- Pain with gripping
- Handshake painful
- Coffee cup sign: pain when lifting a full cup

**Rehab:** Tyler twist (reverse Tyler twist with Theraband FlexBar) — highly effective for lateral epicondylosis. Eccentric wrist extension protocol. Progressive loading. Avoid full wrist flexion under load initially.

**Recovery:** 8–16 weeks. Frequently chronic if not addressed early.
**Severity:** Moderate — see physio for structured loading program
**Can continue climbing:** Modified — avoid stemming, underclings, and positions that load the wrist extensors
**Hangboard clearance:** 3–4 weeks; avoid any grip that reproduces lateral elbow pain
**Campus board clearance:** 12+ weeks

## Distal Biceps Tendinopathy / Partial Tear

**Clinical name:** Distal biceps tendinosis or partial tear
**Mechanism:** Heavy lock-off positions, sustained overhead pulling, campus board, limit bouldering requiring repeated supination under load
**Location:** Front (anterior) of the elbow at the bicipital tuberosity

**Symptoms:**
- Anterior elbow pain
- Pain with resisted supination (turning palm up) and elbow flexion
- Tenderness at the bicipital tuberosity (front of elbow, slightly deep)
- Weakness with supination

**Red flag — Complete distal biceps rupture:** Sudden pop at the front of the elbow, immediate weakness and inability to supinate against resistance, visible Popeye deformity (muscle belly bunches up in upper arm). This is a surgical emergency — referral within 2–3 weeks of injury for best outcomes.

**Recovery:** Tendinopathy: 8–12 weeks. Partial tear: 12–20 weeks. Complete rupture: surgical repair required.
**Severity:** Tendinopathy: moderate. Partial tear: severe. Complete rupture: emergency surgical referral.
**Can continue climbing:** Tendinopathy only — modified, no lock-offs or campus
**Hangboard clearance:** 6–8 weeks for tendinopathy; 12+ weeks for partial tear
**Campus board clearance:** 4–6 months; not before full pain-free supination strength is restored

## Cubital Tunnel Syndrome (Ulnar Nerve Entrapment)

**Clinical name:** Ulnar nerve entrapment at the cubital tunnel, medial elbow
**Mechanism:** Sustained elbow flexion during pulling moves, direct pressure on the medial elbow, swelling from medial epicondylitis compressing the nerve
**Location:** Medial elbow — the nerve passes through the cubital tunnel just behind the medial epicondyle

**Symptoms:**
- Tingling and numbness in the ring finger and pinky finger
- Weakness of intrinsic hand muscles (grip and pinch) in chronic or severe cases
- Pain at the medial elbow that travels down the forearm
- Symptoms worse with elbow in flexed position
- Positive Tinel's sign: tapping over the cubital tunnel reproduces tingling into the ring and pinky fingers

**Red flags:** Progressive intrinsic muscle wasting (hand looks flat between tendons), persistent motor weakness — requires nerve conduction study and specialist referral urgently

**Recovery:** Mild: 4–8 weeks with nerve gliding exercises and activity modification. Severe: surgical transposition may be needed.
**Climbing modifications:**
- Avoid sustained elbow flexion positions
- No campus board
- Pad the elbow during belaying
- Avoid sleeping with the elbow bent (use a pillow to hold elbow straight at night)

**Severity:** Mild: modify. Moderate/severe (progressive weakness or muscle wasting): urgent specialist referral
**Can continue climbing:** Modified — avoid sustained elbow flexion
**Hangboard clearance:** Avoid until tingling resolved
**Campus board clearance:** Last to return; only after full nerve symptom resolution

## Posterior Elbow Impingement

**Location:** Back of the elbow (olecranon region)
**Mechanism:** Repeated full elbow extension under load — mantling, dead hangs, straight-arm compression moves
**Symptoms:** Pain at the back of the elbow at end-range extension, sometimes a clicking sensation
**Recovery:** 4–8 weeks. Avoid forced elbow extension and deadhang positions.
**Severity:** Mild to moderate

---

## Red Flags — Seek Evaluation (All Elbow Injuries)

- Sudden pop or snap with immediate weakness or deformity (possible biceps rupture — surgical emergency)
- Tingling, numbness, or progressive weakness in hand or fingers
- Significant swelling after acute injury
- Night pain that consistently disturbs sleep
- Symptoms not improving after 4–6 weeks of load reduction
- Visible muscle wasting in the hand

---

## Conservative Load Management

**Acute phase (first 1–2 weeks):**
- Reduce climbing volume significantly — avoid steep terrain, campusing, and max-intensity sessions
- Avoid moves that reproduce sharp elbow pain; keep pain below 3/10
- Gentle active range of motion — avoid forced stretching into pain

**Subacute phase (2–6 weeks):**
- Isometric loading is typically well tolerated early
- Gradually reintroduce pulling volume on moderate terrain
- Antagonist training (wrist extension, push-ups, shoulder press) helps restore balance

**Return to full training:**
- Reintroduce board sessions and max-intensity pulling only when pain-free through normal climbing for 2+ weeks
- Campusing and limit bouldering are the last to return
- Eccentric loading programs (under physio guidance) have strong evidence for chronic tendinopathy

| Condition | Estimated Recovery |
|---|---|
| Mild medial tendinopathy | 4–8 weeks |
| Moderate medial tendinopathy | 2–4 months |
| Chronic tendinopathy (>3 months) | 4–6+ months with structured rehab |
| Lateral epicondylitis | 8–16 weeks |
| Distal biceps tendinopathy | 8–12 weeks |
| Distal biceps partial tear | 12–20 weeks |
| Distal biceps rupture | Surgery + 4–6 months |
| Cubital tunnel (mild) | 4–8 weeks |
